So imagine it starting with a student who’s already been talking about integrity. At the end of that conversation you’d say, “Right, this person we’ve been discussing – would you ask them if they’d be available for an interview?” Say it’s their lecturer. The student contacts the lecturer, the lecturer agrees, and then we interview the lecturer about how they’ve learned or taught integrity, and who has influenced them. Then the same question goes to that lecturer about someone else, and so on.

It becomes this branching, slightly random storytelling process, where people are effectively driving the story forward themselves. That’s actually really valuable, because the path the story takes is shaped by their real relationships and experiences, not something we’ve scripted.

We do have to be absolutely clear that we don’t interview children, so nobody gets any funny ideas about involving under‑18s. It’s strictly adults.

Also, I think the theme needs to be broader than just “academic integrity”. It’s really about integrity in general – core to everything we do and say. We can narrow it back down by linking it to a concrete problem, like students using ghost writers to write their assignments. That’s not only an integrity issue, it affects all sorts of other stakeholders. By getting people to talk explicitly about what’s going on there, what integrity means to them, and what options are available, we can start to generate possible solutions. That’s how I’d begin to frame the overall theme.
