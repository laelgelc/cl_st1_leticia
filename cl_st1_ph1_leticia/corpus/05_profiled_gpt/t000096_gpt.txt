That’s why I like to sketch it out: three major acts, across the paper, so you can literally see where everything sits. The middle, that second act, is usually the longest stretch – you’ve got all the montage-y stuff in there, the development, the complications – and that’s very often where filmmakers go wrong. They either pad it out with nothing much happening, or they lose the thread of what the story’s actually doing.

Alongside that, you want to draw in the character arc as a really clear line, a trajectory. It needs to be visible and obvious that this person is going somewhere, not just drifting. By the end, something significant has to have shifted for them – not a tiny, subtle wobble, but a change the audience can really see. You want people to come away thinking, “Right, something amazing has happened to that character,” and they can point to it. So putting that arc on paper, over the three acts, helps you check that there is that big, clear movement in who they are, not just in what’s happening around them.
