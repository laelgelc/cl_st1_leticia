In the seventies there was this American feminist book called *Our Bodies Ourselves* that was hugely influential for me. One of its big themes was being very, very against hysterectomies – the line was that a lot of them were unnecessary, male‑driven surgery, doctors whipping out women’s wombs when they didn’t really need to. I absorbed that quite deeply, so when it came to my own situation I was really resistant to the idea of a hysterectomy for a long time. It definitely coloured how I approached the whole medical process and delayed me in even considering it as an option.

At the same time, I have to say it was an extraordinary resource. Even if, medically, some of it may not stand up now, it had these incredibly detailed diagrams, the proper terminology, lists of symptoms, and, crucially, women’s own accounts of their bodies and of sex. It was like a huge, radical medical handbook specifically for women’s health problems, written from women’s perspectives rather than doctors talking down to you. For the seventies it was really quite radical, just to have all that information laid out plainly and to treat women’s experiences as valid medical knowledge in their own right.
