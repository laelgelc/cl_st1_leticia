What I’m really getting at is that these moving image case studies have value precisely because they’re real and co‑created, not something we’ve just invented on paper. When you’ve got actual people, in an actual situation, working through something like collaboration, it becomes a resource that feels authentic to both the instructor and the learners.

That authenticity is what higher education institutions are struggling to produce themselves. They can’t keep up with how quickly new themes and practices are emerging, and they often don’t have the skills or time to create this sort of rich, filmed material. So we step in with something that’s already been developed, with the narrative and the visuals all in place, and that’s why you can sell it at a premium.

These case studies can then be dropped straight into tutorials or online units. You don’t just give them a video; you wrap it with integrated teaching and learning resources so it’s easy to use. The tutor has a clear example to work from, and the learners have something concrete and engaging to respond to, rather than abstract theory or a dull written scenario.

It’s especially powerful for online students, who often miss out on that sense of “real life” in their learning. A filmed case of people actually collaborating, for instance, gives them a vivid, shared reference point they can discuss, analyse, and apply, and it works just as well for the teacher as for the students.
