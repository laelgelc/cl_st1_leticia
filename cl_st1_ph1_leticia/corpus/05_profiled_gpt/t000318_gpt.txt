I keep coming back to how much of this traces back to Thatcher and Reagan, you know – that whole wave of neoliberal capitalism they kicked off. It feels like they really set this thing in motion that’s just rolled on and on and done so much damage to the world. But then I catch myself wondering if, in a strange way, you need that kind of extreme selfishness and greed to push things to a breaking point, to force a proper reaction on a global scale.

You can see little signs of that in people like Corbyn in the UK and that fella in the States – Bill Sanders – who are basically standing up and saying, look, you’ve got to take responsibility, you’ve got to fight the corruption, you can’t just sit back and let this carry on. It feels like they’re trying to wake people up, to get them to see that the system as it stands is rotten.

It reminds me a bit of the late eighties, when communism just suddenly collapsed. For years it looked solid and immovable, and then almost overnight it just crumbled. I can’t help thinking we might be at a similar kind of turning point now with this particular form of capitalism. Maybe these systems really do have to get as bad as they possibly can before they finally give way and fall apart. There’s something about that pattern – reaching the absolute limit and then suddenly everything shifts – that feels very familiar when you look at what’s happening now.
