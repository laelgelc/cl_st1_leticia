Don’t wait for some perfect, polished question. Just say, “I’m not sure I’ve got this,” or, “This bit’s really interesting – can I ask about…?” And one of the best ways of doing that, without sounding like you’re showing off what you already know, is to ask, “What are the main misconceptions about this topic?”

That question does loads of work at once. It opens things up into a proper three‑way or four‑way dialogue instead of just you and the lecturer batting ideas back and forth. People who know nothing get to hear the really common mistakes and misunderstandings. People who know a bit already can check they’re not falling into those traps. And you get to engage at the level you need without it sounding like, “Please confirm that I’m right.”

It also flips that usual “tell me what I want to hear so I can tick the box” dynamic. You’re not asking them to validate you; you’re asking them to think around the subject. For you in particular, that’s important, because you do need that intellectual challenge. Otherwise you’re going to sit there bored stiff while half the room is still trying to get their heads round the basics. This way, you’re using your questions to pull the whole group up, rather than just marking yourself out.
