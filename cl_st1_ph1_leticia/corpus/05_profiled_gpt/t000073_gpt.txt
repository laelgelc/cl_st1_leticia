and the thing was, for a lot of the girls school should have been a bit of an escape from what was going on at home, somewhere they could feel safe and focus on their work, but it just wasn’t. They were being preyed on by the boys, hassled and intimidated, so instead of being a refuge it turned into a complete nightmare for them.  

And when you look at where some of this comes from, a lot of those lower‑status Sunni Pakistani families, they were originally peasant farmers, displaced and then resettled here about thirty years ago, with no real tradition of formal education or regular work behind them. Over time they’ve become second‑ and third‑generation communities, but the attitude in many of those families hasn’t really shifted. The parents didn’t have education themselves, they’ve managed without it, and so they just don’t see any reason why their children should bother with it either. The youngsters look at their mums and dads and think, well, they’ve got by, so what’s the point?
