When I came over from New Zealand to England, the whole land surveying thing just didn’t translate in the same way. Back home – and in Australia – “surveyor” means something quite specific and quite important: you’re setting out land, you’re involved in exploration, you’re doing all that core spatial and engineering work. It’s a very defined professional role.

Here, “surveyor” seemed to be this catch‑all term. You’ve got building surveyors, land surveyors, electrical surveyors, insurance surveyors, people who are basically plumbers or doing inspections being called surveyors. It’s spread across all sorts of trades and professions. So the job I’d been trained for, and the context I understood, just didn’t really exist in the same way. A lot of what we’d been trained in back in New Zealand simply didn’t apply here in the UK context, or at least not in the form I knew.
