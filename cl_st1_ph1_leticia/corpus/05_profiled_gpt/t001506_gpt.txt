well one of the things that sits behind all of that, particularly with the overseas students and the changing visa rules, is that you can’t possibly do the job properly unless you’re constantly updating yourself. We had to be out visiting employers on a very regular basis, finding out what they were actually doing, what their recruitment practices were, how they were interpreting things like the post‑study work visa, because the legislation and the Home Office guidance seemed to change every five minutes. So you’re forever checking, going on training courses, briefings, that kind of thing, specifically about post‑study work and the legal framework, so that when a student came in you weren’t giving them information that was already out of date.

And it’s not just done individually, there’s a very strong national organisation of university careers advisers, and that’s absolutely central to keeping everybody up to speed. They don’t just have meetings for the sake of it; they actually commission and carry out job studies on a systematic basis. Those studies are then written up and put into a central database, which everyone in the university careers services can use.

To make that work properly, the universities have to agree to release the copyright on the material their staff produce, so it doesn’t sit in a little silo as “ours”, it becomes part of a shared professional resource. The result is that instead of each institution having half a dozen random profiles they’ve cobbled together, you end up with seven or eight hundred, and I wouldn’t be surprised if it’s nearer a thousand job profiles now.

They’re very detailed, and because they’re being revised and added to all the time, they help keep our professional expertise current. Students can go in and look at a proper, up‑to‑date profile of a role they’re interested in, and we as advisers can be reasonably confident that what we’re telling them reflects what’s actually happening out there in the labour market, rather than what it was like ten years ago.
