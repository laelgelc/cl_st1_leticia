In that job I was really trying to get academics to be more aware of their students, and to get students to see academics as human beings too. I ended up acting as a kind of intermediary. With the students, I’d say, look, the academics don’t always know exactly what you’re thinking, and they actually struggle with marking sometimes; they’re not sitting there gleefully failing you, they’re wrestling with what you’ve produced and how to judge it. 

Then with the academics I’d say, your students are often really unsure where they stand, they don’t know how well they’re doing or what they’re supposed to be learning, and they need more clarity and reassurance. I kept trying to get across that, fundamentally, both sides are on the same side – they both want the students to learn and do well – but the way the system is set up positions them as if they’re in opposition. 

And even lecturers who genuinely like and empathise with their students can get pulled into that oppositional dynamic without quite realising it.
