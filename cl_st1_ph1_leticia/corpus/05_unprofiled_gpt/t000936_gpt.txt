well imagine it actually starting with a student who’s been in one of these situations, you know, they’ve thought about integrity in their work, and they decide to email their lecturer and say, “look, there’s this project about integrity and storytelling, would you be willing to be interviewed?” and then the lecturer comes back and says yes, and suddenly you’ve got this whole other strand. then in the interview you’d ask them the same kinds of questions: how did you learn about integrity, or academic integrity? who shaped that for you? who influenced you? how have you tried to teach it? and from there they might mention other people, and you’d follow those threads too.

that’s why I called it branching and a bit random – you don’t know which way it’s going to go, or who it’s going to bring in, but that’s actually the value of it. it’s like a story network growing outwards. the only hard line we need to keep in there is that we don’t interview children at all – that has to be crystal clear so nobody gets any odd ideas about involving kids.

and I don’t think we should keep it boxed in as “academic integrity” only. it’s really integrity in a broader sense, which obviously includes academic stuff like ghost writing – students getting other people to write their assignments – but it’s also about what that does to trust, to institutions, to everyone involved. by having people talk explicitly about those problems, we might surface practical ways of dealing with them, and that then becomes the backbone of the whole theme.
