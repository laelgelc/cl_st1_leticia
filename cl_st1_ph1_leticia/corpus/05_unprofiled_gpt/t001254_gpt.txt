I do genuinely find that whole system really interesting, partly because it gives the director of studies such a clear window into what’s going on in every classroom. If all the teachers are using the same layout on the board, the same way of presenting vocabulary and aims, then when a student comes to complain about a particular teacher, the director actually has something concrete to go on. They know what’s supposed to be happening in every lesson, so they can say, “Well, this is how it should look,” and check whether that’s happening or not.

Where they are, it was all put in place through negotiation with the permanent teachers, which makes sense if you’re there all year and you’ve got a stable staff. Everyone can sit down, talk it through, agree on where the vocabulary goes, what colours to use, all of that.

But at a summer school, that kind of process just isn’t really possible. People are in and out, it’s short-term, and you don’t have the luxury of weeks of discussion. So I’m thinking that if I do try to bring some of those ideas in, it’ll have to be more of a top‑down thing: “This is how we’re doing it this summer.”

I’m not entirely convinced the visiting staff from the London school will actually stick to it, though. I can imagine them nodding and then just carrying on as they always have.
