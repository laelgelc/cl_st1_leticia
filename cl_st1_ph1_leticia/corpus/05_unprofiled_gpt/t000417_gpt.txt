what I say to my first–years, and especially the mature students, is really simple: any time you feel genuinely confused or genuinely excited, stick your hand up. Don’t wait for the magic “right” moment. Just say, “Can I ask, what are the misconceptions about this?”  

That particular question does a lot of work. It opens things up into a proper multi‑way dialogue instead of that usual pattern of people trying to feed the lecturer back exactly what they think they’re supposed to say. You’re not going, “Here’s what I already know, please validate me.” You’re flipping it: you’re inviting them to lay out the common traps, the dodgy assumptions, the ways people typically get it wrong.  

And that helps everybody in the room, whatever their level. If you don’t know much yet, you suddenly get a map of where not to tread. If you already know a fair bit, you get to see the contours of the debate, the fault‑lines, rather than just the basics repeated again.  

For you in particular, I think that’d be really good, because you do need that kind of intellectual challenge. Otherwise you’re just going to be sat there bored stiff while other people are still catching up on stuff you’ve already read to death. This way you’re engaging, but you’re not doing the “smartarse in the corner” routine; you’re actually pulling the level of the whole room up.
