just off the courtyard in Sponza Palace in Dubrovnik there's this room that really brings home what the war was like in nineteen ninety-one and ninety-two. It’s full of photographs of the people who died in the siege, and bits of their belongings and other things from that time, and it’s all laid out so starkly that you can’t really romanticise it or treat it like some distant bit of history. It shows you what actually happened to ordinary people.

And the thing is, when you’re on the coast now Croatia feels like this really young, buzzy country, very lively and modern, and you could almost forget there was a war at all. But if you go inland it’s very different. Places like Vukovar, and the surrounding Serb villages, you can still see the damage from the fighting. There’s this sense that underneath the surface there’s still a divide. Before, Croats and Serbs lived quite happily together, mixed in the same places, but now they tend to live separately, in their own areas, and that old easy togetherness just isn’t there anymore.
