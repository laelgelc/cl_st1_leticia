so to really get the story going I think we actually need to open it out beyond just “academic integrity” and talk about integrity more generally, but still keep it anchored in something concrete. if we phrase it in a way that makes people feel they’re part of a bigger conversation about integrity, they’re more likely to say, “oh, you should talk to so‑and‑so as well,” and that’s how it grows.

we’d want to invite people in a way that explicitly encourages them to recommend others for interviews – almost like a chain, where each person naturally thinks of the next person who has influenced them or has a strong view. that’s how the story gets driven forward by the people themselves, instead of us trying to push it.

to make it real and not just abstract, we can link it to a specific problem that everyone recognises – like students using ghost writers. that’s a very tangible issue that immediately raises questions about integrity, responsibility, and how people respond to pressure.

by talking openly about that sort of problem – actually naming it and unpacking it – we can use the discussion itself as a way of generating solutions. people will start suggesting what’s worked for them, what hasn’t, what they wish institutions or colleagues would do differently.

out of those conversations, we can then frame the overall theme: not just “integrity” as a slogan, but integrity as something lived and negotiated in real situations, with real dilemmas like ghost writing at the centre of the narrative.
