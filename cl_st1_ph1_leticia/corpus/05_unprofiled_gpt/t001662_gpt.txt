When I was younger there was this American book, *Our Bodies Ourselves*, that was really influential. It was very, very anti‑hysterectomy – the line was basically that hysterectomies were this unnecessary, male‑driven surgery that doctors pushed on women. That message lodged itself in my head so firmly that, when I was going through my own medical process, it really made me hold back from even considering a hysterectomy. I kept thinking, “No, this is what they do to women, this is what we’re supposed to resist,” and that did affect how I approached treatment.

At the same time, for all its medical limitations, it was an extraordinary resource. It had these incredibly detailed diagrams, all the proper terminology, and clear explanations of symptoms and different women’s health problems. And crucially, it wasn’t just abstract: it included women’s own accounts of their bodies and of sex, written from their perspective. For the 1970s it was a really radical thing – a sort of big, home‑use medical handbook for women, but from a feminist angle. So I’m very aware that it may have misled me a bit about surgery, but it also gave me language, information, and stories that I just wouldn’t have had otherwise.
