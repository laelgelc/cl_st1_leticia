I honestly think now a lot of them would see being on something like Jeremy Kyle as an aspiration in itself – you know, “get on the telly, have a big barney, air all your dirty laundry”, and they’d be proud of it. It’s that whole thing of, “I’ve been on Jeremy Kyle, I’ve had a DNA test on telly,” and they think that’s an achievement. And I just find that really shocking, that that’s become something to aim for. I think it’s really irresponsible broadcasting, actually, because it encourages people to think that behaving like that, and exposing your private life like that, is perfectly normal and even glamorous.

It ties in with what we see at school. I remember taking this one girl on a school trip – she was a difficult kid, but we’d laid everything out very clearly beforehand. We’d told them all: mobiles off, they’ll have set times they can call home, and if there’s an emergency the parents can contact the school or the staff. Everyone knew the rules, it was all in the letters home.

But this girl’s mum just would not stop phoning. She kept ringing and ringing, even though she’d been told exactly how it would work. And then it turned out she didn’t even have a landline, because she hadn’t paid the bill, so her mobile was the only way to get hold of her – and she still ignored all the instructions.

When we got back, she came storming into the car park, absolutely guns blazing, straight away, shouting her head off. She wasn’t interested in what had actually happened, or what we’d said in advance, she just launched into this big public scene. And that’s the sort of thing that gets normalised when you’ve got that kind of TV and that kind of behaviour held up as entertainment.
