oh yes, the uniforms were very expensive, and as officers we had to buy them ourselves. That was just taken for granted, really. When I was an officer cadet I had to choose a tailor, and I actually went to one hardly anyone had heard of. He wasn’t one of the big fashionable names the others were all going to.

It turned out he was the tailor for the most senior officers, though none of us realised that at the time. So there I was, this young cadet turning up at his place, thinking I’d found a sort of obscure little man, and in fact he was dressing all the top brass.

He was marvellous, very kind to me, and he did something that technically he wasn’t supposed to do. He gave me a bright red silk lining in my uniform. It really was bright red, and it absolutely wasn’t permitted according to the regulations. But he did it quietly, just for me, and of course it was all hidden when the jacket was buttoned up, so nobody ever saw it unless I took it off.

That kind of thing was very much part of the old class-based way the Guards worked, with private tailors and little privileges if you knew where to go. I imagine it’s all a good deal more democratic now, and less bound up with those old social distinctions than it was then.
