What I really like about this idea is that it gives us something concrete to show investors: here’s the rough starting point, and here’s how it can be improved and built on. It’s not just a static “product”; it’s a process they can actually see.

The Creative Commons angle on the website is important. We could let people download only the segments of interview videos where they themselves appear, and then edit those clips for their own digital CVs. That way they’re not just listing skills, they’re literally showing how they think and speak, using their own articulated responses as evidence.

The value of those interviews, though, really rests on two things. First, how good the questioning is – whether the interviewer can draw out something meaningful. Second, how coherent, concise, and articulate the interviewee’s answers are. If either of those is weak, the whole thing is less useful.

A lot of academics and teachers keep saying that students are losing the ability to articulate themselves well, and that’s part of what this responds to. By structuring the interviews and making those clips available, we’re encouraging people to practise and present that skill, and giving them a way to demonstrate it directly.
