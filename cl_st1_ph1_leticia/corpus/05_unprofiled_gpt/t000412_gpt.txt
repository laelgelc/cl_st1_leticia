my job in the end really became about standing in the middle, trying to get the two sides to see each other properly. I’d be talking to students and saying, look, you know those academics marking your work? they’re not sitting there effortlessly churning out grades; they’re actually struggling with the marking, trying to work out what’s fair, what the criteria really mean, how to judge you. and then I’d go to the academics and say, your students genuinely don’t know how they’re doing, they don’t know what you think “good” looks like, they’re not sure how to learn in the way you want.

so you’ve got both groups, basically on the same side, but set up as if they’re in opposition – like they’re enemies or something – when actually they’re not. even the lecturers who really like their students and empathise with them, who honestly care, still get caught in that whole dynamic. it’s just the way the system positions everyone, and I was sort of trying to unpick that a bit.
