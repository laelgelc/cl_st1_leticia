so what we’d actually be offering the universities is more than just the content itself. It’s the whole feeling that comes with it. The way we’d set it up, it would look and feel like a really positive, close relationship between students and lecturers – almost like those old Oxford and Cambridge tutorials, you know? Small groups, people really talking to each other, a kind of warm, collegiate vibe rather than some anonymous, giant lecture theatre.

That’s part of the value: we’d be packaging up that sense of intimacy and care in the teaching, and making it visible. The university gets to signal, “look, this is how we work with our students, this is the kind of interaction you can expect.” It’s a sort of branding, but through genuine learning encounters.

And we’d do it in a way that isn’t just ad hoc. It would be a structured, sustainable process for creating these case studies. So: clear formats, clear stages, a repeatable way of doing it so we’re not reinventing the wheel every time. The institution can see exactly what they’re buying into – how the stories are developed, how the sessions are filmed, how the material is shaped for teaching.

Part of that structure would be putting the case studies online in a way that students can preview them. They can get a sense of what the material is like before they’re in the room, or before they sign up, and that’s attractive in itself. It’s transparent and it’s engaging.

And then there’s the participatory side: students could actually be involved in the case studies themselves. Not just passive consumers, but potentially appearing in them, contributing to them, or helping shape the narratives. So the product is both content and process – learning material that showcases a close, tutorial-style relationship, and a clear, ongoing method for making more of it, with students able to see and maybe step into that process.
