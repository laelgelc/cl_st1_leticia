This pole is best interpreted as a **discourse of everyday financial and temporal calculation in personal life**. It is strongly human-driven.

---

## 1. Who drives this pole?

Mean scores:

- human: -0.03  
- profiled_gpt: -0.09  
- unprofiled_gpt: -0.09  

All three groups are slightly negative on average, but humans are *least* negative (closest to zero), i.e. they contribute relatively more to the positive pole than either GPT condition. All 10 top-scoring texts at this pole are human. So:

- **This positive pole is clearly human-driven**, with LLM turns contributing less to this pattern.

---

## 2. Loadings and what they suggest

High positive loadings:

- month (.63)  
- pound (.63)  
- least (.53)  
- moment (.31)

Key points:

- **month** and **pound** are both high and almost identical in loading: this is a strong signal of talk about **time (on a monthly scale) and money (in British currency)**.
- **least** often appears in scalar or minimising expressions: *at least*, *the least*, *at the very least* → language of **thresholds, minima, sufficiency**.
- **moment** (weaker loading) can mark specific points in time (*at the moment*, *for the moment*), again tying into temporal framing.

Taken together, the loadings point to **routine, quantified talk about money over time**, with a focus on minimum amounts, affordability, and temporal frames.

## 3. What the examples show

All examples are human turns; they consistently involve:

- **Money amounts**: *two thousand pounds*, *three hundred pounds*, *eight hundred pound a month*, *thirty thousand pounds*, *five hundred pound a month*, *two hundred pounds*, *two or three grand*, *thousands and thousands of pounds*.
- **Monthly or multi‑month time frames**: *nine ninety-nine a month*, *three months over the summer*, *two and a half months*, *last month*, *two months*, *two months ago*, *three to six months*, *a few months ago*.
- **Life-admin and financial planning contexts**:
  - Pet care plans and vet bills (Excerpt 1)
  - Insurance and licensing (Excerpt 2, 5)
  - Bank charges and overdrafts (Excerpt 6)
  - Saving for a PhD, travel, qualifications (Excerpt 7)
  - Saving for a house deposit (Excerpt 9)
  - Compensation for housing/insurance issues (Excerpt 5)
  - Business funding and bonuses (Excerpt 4)
- **Scalar / minimum language with “least”**:
  - *I would always be the least out of all of us* (social comparison, but still scalar)
  - *save at least thirty thousand pounds* (explicit financial threshold)
  - *well at least you dumped her* (minimum acceptable outcome)

Even when “least” is not about money, it encodes **evaluation in terms of more/less, better/worse, minimum acceptable outcome**, which fits a broader discourse of **assessing sufficiency and adequacy**.

Crucially, these are not abstract economic discussions; they are **embedded in personal narratives**:

- managing debt and charges with the bank
- negotiating insurance coverage
- planning savings to enable life goals (study, travel, property)
- evaluating relationships and self-image in scalar terms

So the discourse is **intimate, experiential financial/temporal management**, not institutional or technical finance.

## 4. Integrating loadings and examples

Giving equal weight to both:

- Loadings highlight **month + pound + least** → a lexical cluster of **time-based money talk with thresholds**.
- Examples show this cluster realised in **conversational stories about everyday financial arrangements and life planning**, often with emotional or evaluative overtones.

This pole therefore encodes a **lexico-grammatical pattern where speakers quantify money over months, set or discuss minimum amounts, and situate these in personal life events** (housing, education, pets, relationships, travel).

The fact that GPT outputs do *not* strongly drive this pole suggests that:

- LLM turns, even when given profiles, are **less likely to reproduce this very specific, messy, small-scale financial life-admin talk** (with concrete British currency, monthly figures, and personal financial struggles).
- Human speakers naturally produce this discourse as part of everyday conversation.

## 5. Possible labels for this positive pole

All labels focus on this pole alone (no versus framing):

1. **Everyday Financial Life-Management Discourse**  
   - Captures the routine, practical management of money and time in daily life.

2. **Monthly Money and Minimum-Threshold Talk**  
   - Highlights the central lexical pattern: *month*, *pound*, *least*.

3. **Personal Budgeting and Life-Planning Discourse**  
   - Emphasises that the financial calculations are tied to life goals and personal circumstances.

4. **Informal Costing and Sufficiency Evaluation**  
   - Reflects the way speakers cost things out and judge what is “enough” (*at least X pounds*, *at least you…*).

Any of these would be defensible. If a single label is needed, a precise and corpus-grounded option is:

> **“Everyday monthly-money and sufficiency discourse”**

because it directly reflects:

- **monthly time framing** (*month*),
- **monetary quantification in pounds* (*pound*),
- **evaluation of minima and adequacy** (*least*),
- and its **everyday, personal, conversational** character, strongly driven by human speakers.
