Interpretation of Factor 6 (positive pole)

1. **Which group drives this pole?**

- Mean scores:
  - profiled_gpt: **0.131**
  - unprofiled_gpt: **0.128**
  - human: **-0.008**

Both LLM conditions are clearly higher than humans, and the **profiled_gpt** texts are marginally highest. So this pole is **LLM‑driven**, especially by the profiled GPT outputs, with humans slightly on the opposite side of the factor.

2. **Linguistic profile of the positive pole**

High positive loadings:

- **bloke (.61)** – strongly colloquial, British, male‑referential, often evaluative (“big bloke”, “this bloke”).
- **anyone (.47)** – indefinite human reference, often in stance/assessment (“hardly ever have anyone in the back”, “don’t get greedy… if you see anyone missing”).
- **(massive (.46))** – colloquial intensifier/evaluator (“massive”, “massive difference”, “massive queue” – not in excerpts but implied).
- **stand (.43)** – overwhelmingly used as **physical stance / spatial positioning** (“standing there”, “stood there”, “stand back”).
- **chat (.42)** – informal talk, both as verb and noun (“chatting away”, “chatted to me”, “having a chat”).
- **(odd (.42))** – evaluative stance, mild negative (“odd”, “nothing odd had happened”).
- **(thinking (.42))** – internal cognition, reflective stance (“I’m just stood there thinking…”, “I keep thinking…”).
- **(minute (.31))** – temporal granularity, often in narrative pacing (“for a minute”, “in a minute”).

Taken together, these loadings point to:

- **Informal, colloquial lexis** (bloke, chat, massive, odd).
- **Embodied, spatially anchored narration** (stand).
- **Subjective stance and evaluation** (odd, thinking, massive, anyone).
- **Interpersonal / conversational framing** (chat, anyone).

3. Evidence from the high‑scoring excerpts

Across the 10 examples (all profiled_gpt), several consistent discourse features emerge.

a) **Embodied, scene‑setting narration**

“Stand/stood/standing” is used to anchor the speaker or others in space:

- “I was just **standing there**, feeling increasingly anxious…”
- “They were **standing there** watching…”
- “we walked past this tea **stand**…”
- “I was just **standing there** yesterday, opposite the X‑ray department, in my little booth…”
- “I’m just **stood there** thinking what’s the point?”

This creates **vivid, in‑the‑moment scenes**, often with the narrator as an onlooker or participant physically located in the space.

b) **Informal, colloquial social world**

“Bloke” is frequent and strongly loaded:

- “this **bloke**, going on and on…”
- “a couple of **blokes** who were very sceptical”
- “big **bloke**, you’d look at him and think…”
- “Another **bloke** clocked the whole thing…”
- “this big, fat American **bloke**…”

This, plus “lad”, “chap”, “fellow”, “lads”, “keeper”, “shopping channel”, “minions” (gaming), football talk, nights out, etc., constructs a **casual, everyday, often male‑centred social milieu**.

c) **Conversational, chat‑framed activity**

“Chat/chatting” is central:

- “carried on **chatting away** with this bloke”
- “I’d been **chatting** loads about tea with this woman”
- “I was in with my tutor, **chatting through** what I’m doing”
- “he just sort of **chatted** to me…”

The core activity is **talk about talk**: recounting who chatted to whom, what was said, how relaxed or awkward it was. This is meta‑conversational narrative.

d) **Subjective stance and evaluation**

The texts are rich in stance markers and evaluations, often mild and colloquial:

- “nothing **odd** had happened at all”
- “feeling increasingly anxious”
- “it’s like a grand a session, which is **mad money**”
- “the whole business is just **mental** when you think about it”
- “it just hit me… it really brought home how fragile everything is”
- “It’s just this **horrible feeling** of knowing before you even start…”
- “Proper **frustrating** and you just feel **helpless** with it.”
- “it was just really **blatant** and made the whole thing feel even more **awkward**.”

The loading word **thinking** is reflected in explicit metacognition:

- “I keep **thinking** what someone properly famous must be on…”
- “I’m just stood there **thinking** what’s the point?”
- “just trying to get it all straight in my head really, working out the next steps…”

So the discourse is not just recounting events; it foregrounds **how the speaker feels and thinks about them**.

e) **Indefinite human reference and generalisation**

“Anyone” appears in ways that generalise or background specific people:

- “I hardly ever have **anyone** in the back anyway…”
- “don’t get greedy. If that enemy comes close… you can kill them easy… Back off if you see **anyone** missing…”

This supports a discourse style that moves between **specific scenes** and **generalised advice or reflection**.

4. Integrating loadings and examples: what discourse is encoded?

If we give equal weight to the loadings (whole corpus) and the examples (top texts), the positive pole of Factor 6 can be characterised as:

- **Colloquial, British, socially grounded narrative**:
  - Lexis like “bloke”, “chat”, “massive”, “odd” indexes informal, often UK‑English conversation.
  - Situations: pubs/nights out, football, gaming, shopping channel gigs, hospital corridors, car test drives.

- **Embodied, scene‑based storytelling**:
  - Frequent “stand/stood/standing” plus spatial detail (“opposite the X‑ray department”, “in front of a couple of blokes”, “standing there watching”, “stand back a bit”) anchor the narrator and others physically.
  - This gives a **cinematic, ‘I was there’** quality.

- **Reflexive, evaluative stance**:
  - “thinking”, “odd”, “massive”, plus rich evaluative language show speakers **interpreting** events, not just listing them.
  - Emotional and moral evaluations (awkwardness, frustration, admiration, anxiety) are foregrounded.

- **Talk‑about‑talk and interpersonal dynamics**:
  - “chat/chatting” and the narrative focus on who said what, who interrupted, who flirted, who ignored whom.
  - The discourse is about **social interaction itself**: being ignored at a help desk, being coached in a game, being hit on in a bar, being sold a car, being heckled at a show.

- **LLM amplification of colloquial narrative style**:
  - The fact that **profiled_gpt** drives this pole suggests that when given socio‑demographic profiles, the model tends to **over‑produce this kind of colloquial, stance‑rich, scene‑based storytelling**, especially with British colloquialisms like “bloke”.

5. Possible labels for the positive pole of Factor 6

Any label should capture:

- informality and colloquial British lexis,
- embodied, spatially anchored scenes,
- interpersonal talk and meta‑conversation,
- subjective stance and evaluation.

Plausible labels (for this pole only):

1. **“Colloquial, stance‑rich social storytelling”**  
   - Highlights informality (“colloquial”), the heavy use of evaluation and internal states (“stance‑rich”), and the narrative focus on social encounters (“social storytelling”).

2. **“Embodied, chat‑centred everyday narrative”**  
   - “Embodied” reflects the centrality of “stand/stood” and physical positioning;  
   - “chat‑centred” foregrounds “chat/chatting” and talk‑about‑talk;  
   - “everyday narrative” captures the mundane, non‑specialist, lived‑experience content.

3. **“Informal British interpersonal anecdote style”**  
   - Emphasises the British colloquial flavour (“bloke”, “lad”), the interpersonal focus, and the anecdotal, story‑like structure.

Any of these would be defensible. If a single label is required, I would prioritise:

**Colloquial, stance‑rich social storytelling**

because it best integrates the lexical evidence (colloquial items like bloke/chat/massive/odd), the embodied and cognitive stance markers (stand, thinking, anyone), and the example texts’ clear focus on narrated social encounters and how the speaker feels and thinks about them.
