Interpretation of Factor 5 (positive pole)

1. **Which group is driving this pole?**

- Mean scores:
  - Human: **+0.037**
  - Unprofiled GPT: **−0.099**
  - Profiled GPT: **−0.126**

Human turns are the only group with a positive mean on f5, and both GPT conditions are clearly negative. The positive pole is therefore **human‑driven**, and the factor is picking out a pattern that is **characteristic of spontaneous human conversation** and under‑represented in the LLM output.

---

2. **Key lexical loadings**

Positive loadings:

- **em (.93)** – very strong loading
- **(odd (.71))** – likely “for some reason” / “for some odd reason” pattern
- **shit (.40)**
- **carry (.34)**
- **bother (.30)**
- **reason (.30)**

Two things stand out:

- **Disfluency / hesitation**: *em* is by far the strongest loading, and it appears repeatedly in the examples.
- **Causal / justificatory language**: *reason* and the parenthetical *odd* (as in “for some reason/odd reason”) point to speakers explaining or rationalising events.
- **Colloquial stance markers**: *shit*, *bother*, *carry* (likely in “carry on”, “carry it”, etc.) suggest informal, evaluative talk, though they don’t surface in the top 10 excerpts.

So lexically, this pole combines:
- **Spoken disfluency** (em)
- **On‑the‑fly reasoning and explanation** (reason, odd reason / for some reason)
- **Informal, mildly emotive vocabulary** (shit, bother)

---

3. Evidence from the high‑scoring excerpts

All 10 high‑scoring texts are **human**. The loading words that actually appear are **em** and **reason**, often in the fixed expression **“for some reason”**.

Patterns across the excerpts:

a) **Heavy use of “em” as a planning device**

Examples:

- “em I really wanted to wait to involve a lot more people…” (t000657)
- “em which I didn't even realise would take off as much as it did…” (t000651)
- “em yeah and my dad regularly used to em for some reason best known to himself…” (t000288)
- “get that in em so I've got that number…” (t000808)
- “we put em in there…” (t001676)

Here *em* is not contentful; it marks **online planning, hesitation, and turn‑holding** while the speaker works out what to say next. It is typical of **unrehearsed, narrative talk**.

b) **Frequent explicit reasoning / justification**

The word **reason** is used to:

- Introduce **motives or causes**:
  - “the reason I came up with this last idea was…” (t000651)
  - “for some reason best known to himself he used to do the garden…” (t000288)
  - “for some reason I put loads of petrol in it well I know why…” (t000051)
  - “what is the erm the reason why he keeps moving along this trajectory?” (t000097)
  - “the reason he's working with is that erm that also gave it a bit more credence…” (t000063)

- Frame **normative or evaluative reasoning**:
  - “that would be reason for you to be disciplined…” (t000081)
  - “for no good reason and how it's men wanting to kind of cut off women's bits…” (t001662)

Speakers are **explicitly articulating causes, motives, and justifications**, often with metalinguistic framing (“the reason is…”, “for some reason…”).

c) **Narrative, anecdotal, and explanatory discourse**

The content of the excerpts is largely:

- **Personal narratives / anecdotes**:
  - Minecraft building mishaps and community projects (t000657, t000651)
  - Family gardening habits (t000288)
  - Phone numbers and synced contacts (t000808)
  - Restaurant / burrito story (t001676)
  - Car, petrol, and insurance logistics (t000051)

- **Explanations and evaluations**:
  - Explaining a story structure / character arc (t000097)
  - Explaining why a son’s business placement “makes sense” (t000063)
  - Evaluating workplace behaviour and disciplinary norms (t000081)
  - Reflecting on feminist medical literature and its consequences (t001662)

These are **highly situated, story‑like accounts** where the speaker is:

- Reconstructing events
- Explaining why things happened
- Evaluating whether they were reasonable or not

d) **“For some reason” as a key pattern**

Several excerpts use **“for some reason”**:

- “cos it was for some reason we decided to build it on the coast” (t000657)
- “for some reason best known to himself he used to do the garden…” (t000288)
- “for some reason I put loads of petrol in it well I know why…” (t000051)

This construction does two things:

- Marks **partial or uncertain knowledge of motives** (“I don’t fully know why”)
- Still frames the narrative in terms of **reasons and causality**

So the discourse is **reason‑oriented**, but also **self‑reflective about gaps in knowledge**.

---

4. Integrating loadings and examples

Taking loadings and examples together:

- **Disfluency and planning** (*em*):
  - Strongest loading, pervasive in examples.
  - Signals **spontaneous, unedited speech**, with speakers thinking aloud.

- **Causal / justificatory framing** (*reason*, “for some reason”, *odd*):
  - Speakers repeatedly **foreground reasons, causes, and motives**.
  - They explicitly label segments of talk as “the reason…”, “for some reason…”.

- **Informal, mildly emotive stance** (*shit*, *bother*, *carry*):
  - Although not visible in these 10 excerpts, their positive loadings suggest that across the corpus, texts high on this factor also contain **colloquial, mildly expletive or evaluative language**, consistent with informal storytelling and complaint.

- **Human vs LLM**:
  - Humans score positively; GPT outputs (profiled and unprofiled) are negative.
  - LLM turns, even when conversational, tend to be **more fluent, less disfluent**, and often **less explicit about “reasons” in this casual, self‑reflective way** (especially the “for some reason” idiom and filled pauses like *em*).

So this pole is not just “spoken vs written”; it is more specifically:

- **Spontaneous, narrative, and explanation‑heavy human talk**, with:
  - On‑the‑fly planning (em)
  - Overt reasoning and justification (reason, for some reason)
  - Informal stance and mild expletives (shit, bother)

5. Possible labels for f5 (positive pole)

Focusing on this pole only, and reflecting both the lexical pattern and the discourse function, plausible labels include:

1. **“Spontaneous Justificatory Narrative”**

   - *Spontaneous*: captures the heavy *em* disfluency and online planning.
   - *Justificatory*: reflects the centrality of *reason* and “for some reason” in explaining and justifying actions.
   - *Narrative*: matches the anecdotal, story‑like nature of the excerpts.

2. **“Hesitant Reason‑Giving Talk”**

   - *Hesitant*: anchored in *em* (.93) and the fragmented, self‑correcting style.
   - *Reason‑Giving*: anchored in *reason* (.30) and the repeated explicit framing of motives and causes.

3. **“Informal Causal Storytelling”**

   - *Informal*: aligns with colloquial items like *shit*, *bother*, *carry*.
   - *Causal*: reflects the focus on reasons, causes, and “for some reason”.
   - *Storytelling*: reflects the anecdotal, event‑recounting structure.

---

6. Summary characterisation

Factor 5 (positive pole) is best understood as a **human‑driven discourse dimension of spontaneous, informal, reason‑oriented storytelling**, characterised by:

- Frequent **filled pauses** (*em*) and other signs of online planning
- Explicit **reason‑giving and causal explanation** (*reason*, “for some reason”)
- **Colloquial stance** and mild expletives (shit, bother, carry) across the wider corpus

Among the proposed labels, **“Spontaneous Justificatory Narrative”** or **“Hesitant Reason‑Giving Talk”** most directly capture both the linguistic loadings and the discourse functions evident in the high‑scoring texts.
