This pole is clearly human‑driven (humans have the lowest mean score on f3: −0.20 vs +0.06/+0.10 for both GPT conditions), so we are looking at a discourse style that humans use more than the LLMs.

### 1. What the loadings tell us

High negative loadings:

- wanna (−.70)
- fact (−.61)
- okay (−.56)
- reason (−.54)
- (shit (−.48))
- (bother (−.30))

Two clusters stand out:

1. **Informal, interactional stance markers**
   - wanna, okay, shit, bother  
   → colloquial, speech‑like, evaluative, managing the interaction (“okay?”) and expressing attitude (“shit”, “bother”).

2. **Explanatory / justificatory metadiscourse**
   - fact, reason  
   → signalling explanation, justification, or argument structure (“the reason why…”, “in actual fact…”).

So lexically, this pole combines:
- highly **spoken, informal, interpersonal** markers, with
- a strong **explanatory / reasoning** orientation.

### 2. What the examples show

All top‑scoring texts are human. Across very different topics (relationships, pedagogy, entrepreneurship, gaming, politics, family finances), the same pattern recurs:

#### a) Ongoing explanation and justification

Speakers repeatedly frame what they’re doing as giving reasons, accounts, or clarifications:

- “there is one reason only and that's cos…” (t000687)
- “the reason why a case study is useful is because…” (t000938)
- “what you wanna do… okay? … what is the erm the reason why he keeps moving along this trajectory?” (t000097)
- “these are the reasons why I'm going in this direction…” (t000124)
- “in actual fact the money is is not really what you're chasing so I ask you the question why…” (t000193)
- “for some reason they wouldn't do it…” (t000913)

Speakers are not just narrating events; they are **accounting for them**, probing motives, causes, and underlying “why”.

#### b) Interactive, listener‑oriented scaffolding

“okay” and “wanna” are used to manage the listener’s engagement and to soften directives:

- “okay actually this has got enough meat on it to become a case story… do you wanna co‑create a case study…?” (t000938)
- “okay? … then you have to choose what really is the kernel of this person?” (t000097)
- “well okay I can run with those things…” (t000124)
- “okay this case study may not come university but the fact that you've putting it in your online course…” (t000942)
- “yeah okay they're all nicely lined up… you got your R now wanna use that…” (t000791)
- “on Monday okay? and then we have two weeks…” (t000051)

This is **highly interactive, didactic talk**: checking understanding, pacing the explanation, inviting participation (“do you wanna…?”).

#### c) Informal, evaluative, and affective stance

Even in serious or abstract topics, speakers use informal stance markers:

- “doesn't wanna bang me… not even fucking kissed me…” (t000687)
- “warm touchy friendly feeling… you're selling a feeling… like Oxford and Cambridge where you used to go round for a cup of tea…” (t000942)
- “you don't need another reason for Americans to dislike you… are you okay with that?” (t001619)
- “oh my god somebody's come and asked me to go and have a coffee with them…” (t000942)

Where “shit” and “bother” occur in the wider corpus, they will similarly mark **emotional reaction / irritation / evaluation**, reinforcing the stanceful, subjective flavour of this pole.

#### d) Expository / advisory genres in spoken form

Several excerpts are essentially **spoken mini‑lectures or coaching sessions**:

- Explaining case studies and course design (t000938, t000942)
- Explaining narrative structure and character arcs (t000097)
- Explaining entrepreneurial learning and career transitions (t000124, t000193)
- Coaching in a game (“use your Q… you got your R now wanna use that…”) (t000791)
- A long, rhetorically structured open letter about same‑sex marriage and Muslim identity (t001619)

These are **expository and advisory**, but delivered in a **highly conversational, informal register**.

### 3. Who drives the pole?

- Humans: mean f3 = −0.195 (lowest)
- Profiled GPT: +0.097
- Unprofiled GPT: +0.063

So this negative pole is **strongly human‑driven**. GPT outputs, even when profiled, are on the opposite side of the factor, suggesting they:

- use fewer of these colloquial markers (“wanna”, “okay”, “shit”, “bother”), and/or
- structure explanation with more formal devices rather than “fact”, “reason” in this colloquial way.

In other words, this pole captures a **distinctively human conversational explanatory style** that the LLMs under both prompts approximate less.

### 4. Interpreting the discourse dimension

Putting the lexical and textual evidence together, this pole encodes a discourse style that is:

- **Spoken and informal** (wanna, okay, shit, bother)
- **Strongly explanatory / justificatory** (fact, reason)
- **Interactive and listener‑oriented** (okay? do you wanna…?)
- **Stanceful and affective**, often embedding evaluation and personal perspective within the explanation.

It is not just “informal speech” and not just “exposition”; it is **informal, interpersonal explanation and reasoning in conversation**.

### 5. Possible labels for this pole

Any label should capture both the **explanatory/justificatory function** and the **informal, interactive, human‑conversational style**. Suitable options:

1. **Conversational Explanation and Justification**
   - Highlights the central role of “reason”, “fact”, and the pervasive giving of accounts, while signalling that this is done in a conversational, not formal, mode.

2. **Informal Explanatory–Stance Discourse**
   - Emphasises the mix of explanation (“fact”, “reason”) with stance and informality (“wanna”, “okay”, “shit”, “bother”).

3. **Interactive, Reason‑Giving Talk**
   - Focuses on the interactional management (“okay?”, “do you wanna…?”) and the recurrent practice of offering reasons and justifications.

Of these, **“Conversational Explanation and Justification”** best captures what this negative pole represents in the corpus:

- “Conversational” → human‑like, informal, with “wanna”, “okay”, mild expletives.
- “Explanation and Justification” → the heavy use of “reason”, “fact” and the pervasive giving of accounts, advice, and rationales across topics.
