This pole is driven by **profiled GPT** texts (mean −0.14 vs humans +0.31), so it characterises the *LLM-with-profile* responses rather than human turns. Unprofiled GPT is also slightly negative, but the strongest separation is human (positive) vs both GPT groups (negative), with profiled GPT most negative.

### 1. Linguistic profile of the pole

High negative loadings:

- **odd (−.78)**
- **sense (−.62)**
- **seem (−.33)**
- **pick (−.32)**  
- plus weaker: horrible, chat, change, awful, moment

Key observations from the loadings and their use in the excerpts:

1. **Meta‑cognitive / interpretive lexis**
   - *sense* is almost always used in an abstract, interpretive way:
     - “it’s not like a mystery in that **sense**”
     - “in that **sense** the town hall lot are quite a strong possibility”
     - “I’m not really bothered in that **sense**”
     - “the idea is that the sheet makes **sense** to them”
     - “this **sense** of giving outwards”
     - “in the academic **sense**”
   - This is classic discourse of *framing, categorising, and re‑describing* situations.

2. **Epistemic hedging and appearance vs reality**
   - *seem / seemed / seeming*:
     - “the basics **seem** really clear”
     - “what they mainly **seem** to need”
     - “they never **seem** to mention that bit”
     - “the whole of America **seemed** to have descended”
     - “a lot of lecturers just don’t **seem** able to be generous”
     - “you don’t want to alienate… by **seeming** too challenging”
     - “nobody up there **seemed** to have the faintest idea”
   - These mark **evaluative distance** and **tentative stance**: the speaker is not just reporting events but commenting on how things appear, may be perceived, or are framed.

3. **Markedness and evaluation**
   - *odd*:
     - “that does feel a bit **odd** to me”
     - “not **odd** one‑offs”
     - “which was rather **odd**”
   - *horrible, awful* (though not in these particular excerpts) also point to **evaluative commentary**.
   - These items flag **deviation from expectations** and invite explanation or rationalisation.

4. **Selective attention and extraction**
   - *pick / picking / picked*:
     - “he was **picked** up by the Taliban”
     - “trying to **pick up** as much as I can from them”
     - “I feel like **picking up** the phone”
     - “not the sort of place you’d normally **pick**”
   - Semantically, these are about **selecting, extracting, or being singled out**, often in contexts where the speaker is highlighting particular aspects of a situation for comment.

5. **Discourse function**
   Across the excerpts, these items cluster in stretches where the speaker:
   - explains why something is *odd* or unexpected
   - clarifies “in what sense” something is true
   - distinguishes appearance (*seem*) from underlying evaluation
   - picks out particular features for scrutiny

So lexically, this pole is not about a concrete topic (e.g. travel, teaching) but about a **stance‑taking, interpretive, meta‑explanatory style**.

### 2. Discourse patterns in the high‑scoring excerpts

Across the 10 examples, the same discourse behaviour recurs:

1. **Reframing and qualification**
   - Ex. 1 (soldier/desertion podcast):  
     The speaker reframes the podcast’s premise: “I’m still not really sure what the actual crime is… it’s not like a mystery in that **sense**”. They contrast the *seeming* clarity of facts with the investigative framing.
   - Ex. 2 (ex‑partner seeing someone new):  
     The speaker carefully qualifies social norms: “that does feel a bit **odd** to me… it makes more **sense** in my head if… without it **seeming** strange.”
   - Ex. 3 (room‑booking / town hall):  
     “In that **sense** the town hall lot are quite a strong possibility… what they mainly **seem** to need is… not **odd** one‑offs.” The speaker is modelling others’ needs and rationalising a plan.

2. **Meta‑commentary on one’s own reasoning**
   - Ex. 4 (career change / entrepreneurship):  
     The speaker explicitly lays out their logic: “I’m not really bothered in that **sense**… I can at least lay out the logic… I’m changing direction, I’m in a transition…” with “trying to **pick up** as much as I can”. This is self‑explanation and justification.
   - Ex. 5 (teaching method):  
     “The idea is that the sheet makes **sense** to them… I just write things in a way that I know they’ll remember.” Again, explicit reasoning about method.

3. **Critical, reflective evaluation of institutions and others**
   - Ex. 6 (unions and wages):  
     “They never **seem** to mention that bit…” The speaker contrasts media narratives with their own interpretation, wanting to “**pick** up the phone” and correct them.
   - Ex. 8–9 (university teaching, mature students):  
     “They tend to put the weakest… when actually…”; “this **sense** of giving outwards”; “a lot of lecturers just don’t **seem** able to be generous”; “you don’t want to alienate… by **seeming** too challenging… in the academic **sense**.” These are reflective critiques of academic culture and perceptions.

4. **Narratives framed through perceived oddity or misalignment**
   - Ex. 7 (Florida trip, dodgy trailer park):  
     “Not the sort of place you’d normally **pick** if you had any choice… it was all right, really.” The narrative is organised around expectations vs reality.
   - Ex. 10 (museum exhibition):  
     “It was as if the exhibition didn’t exist, which was rather **odd**… nobody up there **seemed** to have the faintest idea…” The story is about institutional disorganisation, framed as puzzling.

Across topics (war, relationships, teaching, travel, museums), the **common discourse function** is:

- stepping back from events
- evaluating how they *seem* vs how they *should* be
- clarifying “in what sense” something holds
- highlighting oddities and misalignments
- justifying one’s own interpretation or course of action

This is a **reflective, interpretive, mildly critical stance** rather than purely narrative or purely interpersonal.

### 3. Who drives this pole?

- All 10 high‑scoring excerpts are **profiled GPT**.
- Mean scores:
  - humans: +0.31 (i.e. away from this negative pole)
  - profiled GPT: −0.14
  - unprofiled GPT: −0.12

So this negative pole is **strongly associated with LLM‑generated, especially profiled, turns**. It appears that when GPT is given a socio‑demographic profile, it tends to produce more **overtly interpretive, meta‑explanatory, stance‑laden** language of this kind than humans do in the original conversations.

### 4. Proposed labels for this pole

Any label should capture both:

- the **lexical signature** (odd, sense, seem, pick, horrible, awful, chat, change, moment)
- the **discourse behaviour** (interpretation, qualification, evaluation, explanation of reasoning)

Possible labels:

1. **Reflective Interpretive Stance**
   - Captures the pervasive use of *sense* and *seem* to frame how things are understood, and the tendency to comment on perceptions, norms, and reasoning.

2. **Meta‑Explanatory Evaluation**
   - Emphasises that speakers are not just evaluating (odd, awful, horrible) but also explaining *in what sense* and *how things seem*, often justifying their own or others’ actions.

3. **Perceived‑Oddity and Sense‑Making Discourse**
   - Brings in *odd* and *sense* explicitly: the discourse is organised around noticing what is odd or misaligned and then making sense of it.

4. **Analytical Commentary Style**
   - A more general label highlighting that these turns function as commentary on events, institutions, or relationships, rather than simple recounting.

If I had to choose one primary label for this pole, grounded in both the loadings and the examples, I would propose:

> **Reflective Sense‑Making and Evaluative Commentary**

- **Reflective**: speakers step back from events and from their own actions.
- **Sense‑Making**: anchored in the high loading of *sense* and frequent “in that sense / in the academic sense” constructions.
- **Evaluative Commentary**: anchored in *odd, seem, horrible, awful, pick* and the recurrent pattern of judging situations, institutions, or social arrangements, often with mild criticism or puzzlement.

This label captures the discourse dimension encoded at this negative pole and aligns with the fact that it is particularly characteristic of **profiled GPT** turns, which appear to over‑produce this kind of explicit, rationalising, interpretive talk.
