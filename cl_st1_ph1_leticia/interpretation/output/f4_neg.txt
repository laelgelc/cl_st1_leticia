This pole is very sparse lexically (only two loading items), so the interpretation has to lean heavily on how those items pattern in the corpus and in the examples.

## 1. What is lexically driving this pole?

Negative loadings:
- **minute** (−.42)
- **fact** (−.38)

In the 10 highest‑scoring texts on this negative pole, only **minute** appears as a loading word, and it is always used in its **time‑measurement** sense:

- “fifteen or twenty **minutes** on the bus”
- “every five **minutes** she was calling her”
- “thirty **minutes**”
- “only be five **minutes** late”
- “they can’t walk more than ten **minutes**”
- “I’ve gotta get up in a **minute**”
- “talk for about five **minutes**”
- “waiting for her for fifteen **minutes**”
- “five **minutes** later the email comes through”
- “they lasted like fifteen **minutes**”

So the lexical signal here is **fine‑grained, clock‑based time reference**: minutes, five minutes, fifteen minutes, thirty minutes, in a minute, etc.

We don’t see **fact** in these particular excerpts, but its co‑loading suggests that, across the full corpus, this pole also attracts uses of *fact* (likely in discourse‑organising or evidential functions: “in fact”, “the fact is”, “the fact that…”).

## 2. What kind of discourse do the examples instantiate?

All 10 examples are:

- **Human** turns (no GPT examples among the top negatives).
- **Narrative or anecdotal** stretches of talk.
- Focused on **very specific, short time spans** and **micro‑sequencing of events**.

They share several discourse properties:

1. **Micro‑temporal structuring of experience**

Speakers repeatedly break events down into small, measured units:

- Commuting and lunch: “fifteen or twenty minutes on the bus… ten fifteen minutes… half an hour at home”
- Phone calls: “every five minutes she was calling her”
- Travel mishaps: “only be five minutes late”
- Medical/functional limits: “they can’t walk more than ten minutes”
- Waiting and coordination: “waiting for her for fifteen minutes… we missed each other for forty‑eight hours”
- Service interactions: “five minutes later the email comes through”
- Entertainment: “they lasted like fifteen minutes… everyone was waiting there for an hour or more just to see fifteen minutes”

The discourse is organised around **how long things take**, **how often they recur**, and **exact timing** of key moments.

2. **Everyday, lived‑experience narratives**

The topics are mundane but consequential in everyday life:

- Work and commuting
- School trips and parental behaviour
- Medical treatment and disability
- Relationship tensions
- Training courses and presentations
- Customer service complaints
- Fireworks displays

These are **personal stories** about “what happened to me / to someone I know”, not abstract discussion.

3. **Temporal precision as stance and evaluation**

The minute‑level detail is not just descriptive; it often carries **evaluation**:

- “seems crazy” (after describing the lunch‑break timings)
- “it was just a nightmare”
- “it is really… quite depressing”
- “oh what a hassle”
- “they lasted like fifteen minutes… they weren’t even… it was just a bit creepy”

The precise timing helps **build a case**: that something was unreasonable, exhausting, depressing, or disappointing. Minute‑by‑minute narration functions as **evidence** for the speaker’s stance.

4. **Local sequencing of events**

The narratives are tightly sequenced:

- “five seconds after I’d kicked off on the phone five minutes later the email comes through”
- “she got the wrong ticket… she had to get a taxi… there was a lot of traffic… how she managed to only be five minutes late I still don’t know”
- “I was like going in for a cuddle… I’ve gotta get up in a minute… he was just like oh just leave me alone… so I got up… so I’m leaving you alone”

Time expressions anchor **event order** and **causal chains** in a very local, short‑span way.

## 3. Which group is driving this pole?

Mean scores on Factor 4:

- Human: **0.33**
- Unprofiled GPT: **0.80**
- Profiled GPT: **0.83**

Higher mean = more positive on the factor; the **negative pole** is therefore **least characteristic of GPT** and **most characteristic of humans**.

All 10 highest‑scoring negative texts are **human**. So:

- The **negative pole** is **human‑driven**.
- GPT outputs (both profiled and unprofiled) tend to be **pulled away from this pole**, i.e. they use **less minute‑level temporal detailing** and/or less of the co‑occurring “fact” pattern.

In other words, this pole captures a **style of human conversational storytelling** that GPT is comparatively less likely to reproduce.

## 4. Interpreting the discourse dimension at this pole

Putting the lexical and discourse evidence together:

- Lexically: dominated by **minute‑scale time reference**, with **fact** suggesting a broader evidential/argumentative flavour in the full corpus.
- Discursively: **personal, anecdotal, micro‑temporal narratives** where speakers reconstruct short episodes in fine temporal detail, often to **justify an evaluation** or **make a complaint / express frustration**.

This is not just “narrative” in general; it is **narrative anchored in precise, clock‑time granularity**, used to **account for** or **argue about** everyday experiences.

### Possible labels for this negative pole

Any of the following would be defensible; they foreground slightly different aspects of the same pattern:

1. **“Micro‑temporal, experience‑based narration”**  
   - Captures the minute‑by‑minute structuring of lived events.

2. **“Minute‑by‑minute everyday storytelling”**  
   - Emphasises the clock‑time granularity and the mundane, personal topics.

3. **“Fine‑grained temporal accounting in personal talk”**  
   - Highlights the use of precise time as a way of “accounting for” what happened (especially in complaints and justifications).

4. **“Human‑style micro‑chronicle discourse”**  
   - Stresses that this is a human‑driven way of narrating: short episodes told as micro‑chronicles.

If I had to choose one primary label for this pole, balancing clarity and coverage of both loadings and examples, I would propose:

> **Micro‑temporal everyday storytelling**

Justification:

- **Micro‑temporal**: grounded in the dominant loading *minute* and its use to segment time very finely.
- **Everyday**: topics are ordinary, lived experiences (work, travel, relationships, health, services).
- **Storytelling**: the excerpts are narrative, event‑sequencing accounts, not abstract exposition.

This label also aligns with the source pattern: it describes a **distinctively human conversational mode** that GPT outputs, on average, do not reproduce to the same extent.
