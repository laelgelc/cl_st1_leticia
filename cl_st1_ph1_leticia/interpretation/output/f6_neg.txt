Interpretation of Factor 6 (negative pole)

1. **Which group is driving this pole?**

- Mean scores are all slightly negative, but **profiled_gpt** is the lowest:
  - human: −0.03  
  - unprofiled_gpt: −0.09  
  - profiled_gpt: −0.09 (slightly lower, and all 10 top texts here are profiled_gpt)
- The **high‑scoring texts at this pole are exclusively profiled_gpt**. So this pole is clearly **LLM‑driven, especially under the profiled prompt**.

2. **Linguistic profile of the pole**

Loadings (all negative, i.e. characteristic of this pole):

- anyone (−.73) – very strong
- (massive) (−.41)
- guy (−.40)
- everyone (−.33)
- (shit) (−.30)

Key observations from the loadings:

- **Indefinite / generic human reference**:
  - anyone, everyone, guy
- **Colloquial / informal register**:
  - guy, shit, massive (as an intensifier)
- **Mass/generalising stance**:
  - everyone, anyone → sweeping reference to groups, norms, expectations

3. Evidence from the excerpts

Across the 10 excerpts, the loading items are used in very consistent ways:

a. **Generic and inclusive reference to groups**

- “hard to get **everyone** together” (f6_neg_001)
- “keeping **everyone** entertained” (002)
- “you’re always kind of ‘on’ around **everyone**” (003)
- “in an ideal world **everyone** would have the same opportunities” (004)
- “apparently this new process and system are working really well, **everyone’s** on board” (005)
- “basically **everyone** who’s a lecturer has a PhD” (006)
- “she’s the one who really knows **everyone**” (007)
- “before **anyone** else was even up … after **everyone** in the evening” (008)
- “it only has to snow about half an inch now and **everyone** seizes on it as an excuse” (009)
- “right so **everyone** have a look at your new loyalty card now, but don’t show it to **anyone**” (010)

These uses:

- Construct **collectives** (“everyone”, “loads of people”) and **open sets** (“anyone”).
- Often frame **norms, expectations, or shared experience**:
  - “basically everyone who’s a lecturer has a PhD”
  - “I can’t think of a single person who is [happy with it]”
  - “no one had prepared … it wasn’t as if they didn’t have time”

b. **Colloquial, conversational stance**

- “there’s a Pakistani **guy** who’s about my age” (001)
- “this **guy** called Lockwood” (007)

“guy” is a **casual, relational label** for a person, typical of informal spoken narrative.

Although “massive” and “shit” don’t appear in these particular 10 excerpts, their loadings suggest:

- **massive**: colloquial intensifier (“massive problem”, “massive difference”)
- **shit**: informal / mildly taboo evaluation (“talking shit”, “no one gives a shit”)

Together with “guy”, they point to a **colloquial, everyday‑speech lexicon**.

c. **Discourse functions visible in the examples**

The high‑scoring texts repeatedly:

- **Generalise from individual to group**:
  - “loads of people straightaway were like, ‘no, that’s too much’”
  - “basically everyone who’s a lecturer has a PhD”
  - “people using the tiniest bit of snow as an excuse”
- **Construct shared norms and expectations**:
  - “for that amount of money you sort of expect there to be a bit more thought”
  - “in an ideal world everyone would have the same opportunities”
  - “it just shows how badly it can go wrong when they don’t grit the roads”
- **Position the speaker relative to ‘everyone/anyone’**:
  - “I don’t think anyone should feel proud or ashamed of where they’ve ended up”
  - “I honestly doubt she’s actually asked anyone if they’re happy with it”
  - “you can’t just sit and be, or be openly miserable, because you’re always kind of ‘on’ around everyone”

So the pole is not about technical vocabulary or narrative vs. expository structure; it is about **how people are referred to and grouped**, and the **stance** taken toward those groups.

4. Discourse characterisation

Putting the loadings and examples together, this pole encodes:

- **High use of generic human reference**:
  - “anyone”, “everyone”, “guy” → people as **types** or **members of a group**, not as named individuals.
- **Colloquial, conversational style**:
  - “guy”, “massive”, “shit” → informal, spoken‑like register.
- **Normative and evaluative generalisation**:
  - Frequent talk about what “everyone” does, what “anyone” should/shouldn’t feel, how “people” behave.
  - Used to **evaluate** situations (work systems, snow response, social justice, weekend planning).

This looks like a dimension of **informal, people‑centred, generalising talk**: speakers (here, LLMs emulating speakers) use broad, inclusive pronouns and colloquial person labels to:

- Construct **social groups and shared experience**.
- Make **sweeping claims** about behaviour, norms, or fairness.
- Position themselves against or within those collectives.

5. Possible labels for this pole

Focusing on this pole alone, and reflecting both the lexical loadings and the discourse functions, plausible labels include:

1. **“Colloquial Generic‑People Reference”**  
   - Captures the informal register (“guy”, “shit”, “massive”) and the heavy use of “anyone/everyone” to talk about people in general.

2. **“Informal Collective‑Norm Talk”**  
   - Emphasises that “everyone/anyone” are used to construct collectives and to talk about norms, expectations, and shared behaviour.

3. **“Everyday Generalisations about People”**  
   - Highlights the discourse function: broad, everyday generalisations about what people do, feel, or should do, expressed in a conversational style.

Any of these would be defensible. If a single label is needed, I would choose:

> **Informal Collective‑Norm Talk**

because it integrates:
- **Informal**: “guy”, “massive”, “shit”
- **Collective**: “everyone”, “anyone”
- **Norm Talk**: the recurrent use of these forms to state or contest social norms, expectations, and shared behaviours.

6. Role of the prompting regime

- The fact that **profiled_gpt** dominates this pole suggests that when the LLM is given socio‑demographic profiles, it tends to **lean into colloquial, people‑generalising language** (“everyone”, “anyone”, “guy”) to sound like a plausible conversational persona.
- Humans and unprofiled_gpt are closer to zero on this factor, so this **informal, collective‑norm style is particularly amplified by the profiled prompting**.
