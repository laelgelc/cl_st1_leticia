This pole is driven almost entirely by human speech and is characterised by a small but very coherent lexical cluster:

- fuck (-.74)
- set (-.52)
- somebody (-.51)
- great (-.40)

Human turns have a clearly lower mean score (more negative) than both GPT conditions:

- human: -0.12
- profiled_gpt: +0.06
- unprofiled_gpt: +0.06

So this is a *human‑driven* pole; the LLM outputs sit away from it (they are more neutral/positive on this factor), which fits the fact that GPT is generally less likely to use swearing and this kind of colloquial intensification.

Below I propose labels, then justify them from loadings and excerpts.

---

## Proposed labels for f5 (negative pole)

Possible labels (all for this *single* pole):

1. **“Colloquial, affect‑intensified stance”**
2. **“Human conversational vividness”**
3. **“Informal evaluative storytelling”**

I would prioritise something like:

> **Colloquial, affect‑intensified stance**

because it captures:
- the informal, spoken character (fuck, somebody, set in idiomatic uses)
- the strong evaluative/attitudinal colouring (fuck as intensifier, great as positive evaluation)
- the way speakers take a stance and animate stories.

---

## Justification from loadings and examples

### 1. Lexical loadings

The four loading items are few but semantically and functionally coherent:

- **fuck**  
  - Very strong negative loading, highest magnitude.  
  - Used here as:
    - an intensifier (“dodgy as fuck”, “don’t know fuck all”)
    - a verb (“they fucked her over”)
  - Signals high informality, emotional involvement, and often emphasis or indignation.

- **great**  
  - Positive evaluation, often used colloquially (“that’s really great”, “that would be great”).  
  - In these excerpts it is not formal praise but casual, interpersonal evaluation.

- **somebody**  
  - Refers to non‑specific persons in narratives (“somebody fucking says something”, “somebody did the biopsy”, “somebody’s sitting there going…”).  
  - Often used to construct scenarios, generalised characters, or hypothetical participants in a story.

- **set**  
  - Appears in idiomatic or semi‑fixed expressions:
    - “set foot in the lecture theatre”
    - “a different set of responsibilities”
    - “before even think about setting up a camera”
    - “a personality is ever set”
  - These are narrative/argumentative constructions rather than technical “setting” in a formal sense.

Taken together, these words point to:

- **Spoken, informal register** (fuck, great, somebody)
- **Narrative and scenario‑building** (somebody, set in “set foot”, “set of responsibilities”)
- **Strong stance and evaluation** (fuck as intensifier, great as positive stance marker)

So the lexical profile is not about a topic (e.g. health, cars, universities) but about *how* speakers talk: vividly, evaluatively, and colloquially.

---

### 2. Evidence from the excerpts

All 10 high‑scoring texts are human; none are GPT. They are long, rambling, and highly conversational. Key patterns:

#### a. Swearing as stance and intensification

- “when **somebody fucking says** something in a really interesting way” (t000413)  
- “even if you don't know **fuck all** and if you know shitloads” (t000417)  
- “it made flooding noises so like I knew it was **dodgy as fuck** anyway” (t000043)  
- “they **fucked her over** and she had to go to hospital” (t000483)  
- “I'm **fucking** poor” (t000816)

Functions:
- Intensifying evaluation (dodgy as fuck, fucking poor)
- Expressing frustration or indignation (fucked her over)
- Adding vividness and authenticity to reported experience.

This is classic high‑involvement, colloquial stance‑taking.

#### b. “Great” as casual positive evaluation

- “I was like oh that's really **great**” (t000816)
- “I think for you that would be **great** because you need challenging” (t000417)
- “that's a quite a different **set** of responsibilities really” … “the **greatest** variation is” (t001501 – “greatest” is morphologically related)
- “there's a **great** story about Irving Thalberg” (t000829)
- “I've had this **great** idea” (t000942)

“Great” here is not formal or technical; it is a conversational evaluation of ideas, stories, or situations. It contributes to an evaluative, stance‑rich discourse.

#### c. “Somebody” as generic participant in vivid scenarios

- “when **somebody fucking says** something in a really interesting way” (t000413)
- “the misconception is about **somebody's** sitting there going what is a misconception?” (t000417)
- “and **somebody** did the biopsy on that” (t000005)
- “they obviously had **somebody** else who actually like closes it all” (t000043)
- “if you think you're entitled to it fight for it … then **somebody** who's qualified like a surgeon will say no” (t000483)
- “oh my god **somebody's** come and asked me to go and have a coffee with them” (t000942)
- “I see **somebody** who is evolving” (t001038)

Uses:
- Generic “somebody” to build hypothetical or typical scenarios.
- Non‑specific reference to actors in narratives (somebody did the biopsy, somebody else closes the deal).
- Constructing imagined dialogues and inner speech (“somebody's sitting there going…”).

This supports the idea that this pole encodes **storytelling and scenario‑building** with generic human participants, rather than precise, technical reference.

#### d. “Set” in idiomatic, narrative, or evaluative contexts

- “before you've even **set foot** in the lecture theatre” (t000413)
- “that's a quite a different **set** of responsibilities really” (t001501)
- “before even think about **setting up** a camera” (t000829)
- “I don't think a personality is ever **set**” (t001038)

These are not technical uses (e.g. “data set”) but idiomatic or abstract uses that fit conversational explanation and evaluation.

#### e. Overall discourse style

Across the excerpts we see:

- Long, loosely structured turns with many discourse markers (“like”, “you know”, “erm”, “sort of”).
- Heavy use of reported speech and imagined dialogue:
  - “people go I've actually seen that happen” (t000413)
  - “the lecturer was like oh my god somebody's come and asked me…” (t000942)
  - “I said to my first year students…” (t000417)
- Strong interpersonal orientation and stance:
  - Encouraging, advising, or challenging the interlocutor (t000417, t001038, t000942).
  - Expressing indignation or frustration about institutions (NHS, universities, car dealers).

This is not neutral description; it is **highly involved, evaluative, and affect‑laden** talk.

---

### 3. Which group drives the pole?

- Mean scores show humans are more negative on f5 than both GPT conditions.
- All top‑scoring texts at this pole are human.
- The key loading item “fuck” is extremely unlikely in GPT outputs under normal safety constraints, which will push GPT texts away from this pole.
- GPT outputs, even when “profiled”, tend to avoid strong profanity and may use “great” in more polished, less colloquial ways, and are less likely to use “somebody” in the generic, scenario‑building style seen here.

So this pole is clearly **human‑driven**, and more specifically driven by **informal, emotionally engaged human conversation**.

---

## Why “Colloquial, affect‑intensified stance” fits

- **Colloquial**:  
  - Swearing, “like”, “you know”, long unplanned turns, generic “somebody”.
- **Affect‑intensified**:  
  - fuck as intensifier and expression of anger/frustration; great as positive evaluation; strong emotional colouring of narratives.
- **Stance**:  
  - Speakers are not just recounting events; they are evaluating, advising, challenging, and positioning themselves and others (students, lecturers, doctors, car dealers, universities).

Alternative labels like “informal evaluative storytelling” or “human conversational vividness” also capture aspects of this pole, but “colloquial, affect‑intensified stance” foregrounds both the register (informal, human) and the key functional property (strong stance and affect), which are jointly signalled by the loading items and the excerpts.
