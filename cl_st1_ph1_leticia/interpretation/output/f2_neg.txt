Interpretation of Factor f2 (negative pole)

1. Which group drives this pole?

- Mean scores:
  - Human: +0.88
  - Profiled GPT: −0.16
  - Unprofiled GPT: −0.16  

The negative pole is clearly driven by **LLM-generated turns** (both profiled and unprofiled), while human turns cluster at the opposite (positive) end of the factor. All 10 high‑scoring negative examples are from **profiled_gpt**.

So this pole characterises a **distinctively LLM style of talk**, not the human baseline.

The semantically interpretable core is:

> **huge / massive / big / awful**

These are **high-frequency, colloquial, scalar evaluative adjectives** used to mark:
- physical size/extent
- intensity/degree
- strong stance (positive or negative)

They are not technical or precise; they are **vague, emphatic size/intensity boosters**.

---

3. Evidence from the excerpts

Across the 10 examples, these adjectives are used in very similar ways:

a) Everyday hyperbole and evaluation

- “massive fry-up as my main meal”
- “she’ll eat these huge portions”
- “this massive box, which makes it such a hassle”
- “two big lads at the back, both of them huge”
- “massive mistake”
- “huge mutiny”
- “massive fuss”

These are classic **colloquial hyperbolic evaluators**: not literal measurement, but “a lot”, “very”, “really intense”.

b) Large-scale events and spaces

- “massive St Patrick’s Day street party… just huge”
- “Radio One were doing some big thing”
- “this massive room… huge square storage units”
- “this massive light and fountain exhibition”
- “big sweeping steps… huge fountain”
- “such a huge area and such different landscapes”
- “this massive bowl of sand with these huge dunes”

Here the adjectives construct **vivid, large-scale scenes** – parties, landscapes, buildings, crowds.

c) Strong affect and stance

Even when not explicitly emotional, the adjectives help dramatise the narrative:

- “I thought we’d made a massive mistake”
- “properly projectile vomiting”
- “proper freaky moment”
- “huge mutiny”
- “massive fuss”

They function as **stance markers**, signalling the speaker’s emotional investment and dramatising the story.

d) Informal, conversational register

The adjectives co-occur with other informal features:

- “loads of people”, “properly cheap”, “absolutely hammered”
- “it was that bad”, “properly projectile vomiting”
- “it was a proper freaky moment”
- “it’s mad to think”
- “really up for looking after you”
- “they were absolutely mad”

So the dimension is not just “size” but **colloquial, story-telling evaluation of experiences**.

---

4. Discourse characterisation

Putting the loadings and examples together, this pole encodes a discourse style with these properties:

- **Colloquial, narrative talk** about experiences (travel, nights out, mishaps, anecdotes).
- Heavy use of **vague, scalar adjectives** (huge, massive, big, awful) as:
  - size descriptors (physical scale, quantity, distance)
  - intensity boosters (degree of emotion, seriousness, fun, fear)
- Strong **speaker stance and evaluation**: events are framed as impressive, shocking, scary, brilliant, etc.
- A tendency toward **hyperbolic, dramatized description** rather than neutral, measured description.

Because this is the **negative** end of the factor and is dominated by GPT outputs, it suggests that the model, when simulating conversation, leans into a **highly evaluative, hyperbolic, “bigging up” style** more than humans do in this corpus.

---

5. Possible labels for this pole

Any label should capture: (i) colloquial register, (ii) scalar size/intensity, (iii) evaluative stance.

Plausible labels:

1. **Hyperbolic Size‑and‑Intensity Evaluation**
   - Highlights the core lexical pattern (huge/massive/big) and their function as degree boosters.

2. **Colloquial ‘Bigging‑Up’ Narration**
   - Emphasises the informal, story-telling use of “big”/“massive”/“huge” to dramatise events.

3. **Expansive, Vivid Experience‑Talk**
   - Focuses on how large-scale adjectives are used to make experiences feel expansive and vivid.

4. **Informal Scalar Stance and Exaggeration**
   - Stresses that these are scalar stance markers used for exaggeration in informal talk.

---

6. Recommended primary label

If a single label is needed, a precise, discourse-oriented choice would be:

> **Hyperbolic Colloquial Evaluation of Scale and Intensity**

Justification:

- **Hyperbolic**: captures the frequent exaggeration and dramatization.
- **Colloquial**: reflects the informal, conversational register.
- **Evaluation**: these adjectives are not neutral; they encode stance.
- **Scale and Intensity**: “huge/massive/big/awful” jointly cover physical scale, quantity, and emotional/intensity scaling.

This label aligns with both:
- the **loadings** (size/intensity adjectives as the defining lexical set), and
- the **examples** (LLM-generated, informal narratives that “big up” events, places, and feelings).
