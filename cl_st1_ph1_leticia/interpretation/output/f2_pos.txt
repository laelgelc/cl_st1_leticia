## 1. Linguistic profile of f2 (pos)

High positive loadings:

- **er (.77), erm (.67)** – filled pauses, hesitation markers
- **wanna (.73), gonna (.37)** – reduced, colloquial future/intention forms
- **okay (.46)** – discourse marker, turn management, stance
- **cos (.38)** – reduced causal conjunction “because”
- **(shit (.46))** – mild taboo / expletive (even if not visible in these excerpts)
- **(somebody (.31))** – generic human reference

Taken together, these are classic markers of:

- **Spontaneous, online speech production** (er, erm, cos)
- **Informal, colloquial register** (wanna, gonna, cos, shit)
- **Interactive discourse management** (okay as a boundary/uptake marker)
- **Reference to people in a non‑specific, story‑telling way** (somebody)

So at the level of loadings, this pole encodes **highly informal, disfluent, real‑time spoken interaction**.

## 2. What the excerpts show

All 10 high‑scoring texts are **human** turns, and they illustrate the same cluster of features:

### a. Online planning and disfluency

Across the excerpts, we see dense use of **er/erm** in positions where speakers are:

- Searching for words or planning the next bit:
  - “and I'm thinking this was half-built do you know what I mean? … so I was saying ah yes erm and she said…” (t001192)
  - “button start recording okay that's started recording … so erm and I are going to talk about erm er er something…” (t001643)
- Repairing or re‑starting:
  - “there was pplenty of people walking it I said to do you wanna walk the route ? pouring with rain he said no I said er I said no nor me…” (t001187)
  - “no erm so what? yeah we turned up obviously on the Thursday night…” (t001107)

These are **classic markers of real‑time cognitive processing** and narrative construction.

### b. Informal, colloquial stance and alignment

- **wanna / gonna / cos** appear in casual, interpersonal talk:
  - “do you wanna walk the route?” (t001187)
  - “cos we don't wanna be wandering around in the dark” (t001107)
  - “it's a bit of a stinker cos there are there are a lot of people…” (t000989)
  - “rather than cos she doesn't wanna look bad she won't say like oh I can't make it” (t000031)

These forms index **relaxed, non‑formal style** and **solidarity talk** among peers or family.

### c. Discourse management with “okay”

“okay” is used to:

- Mark **topic or phase transitions**:
  - “okay yeah we'll put in if we do need to” (t001192)
  - “alright okay no worries so I tell you what…” (t000691)
  - “so erm and I are going to talk about… okay that’s started recording…” (t001643)
- Signal **acceptance / alignment**:
  - “well okay then then no” (t000989)
  - “you may feel okay with having gay acquaintances or coworkers…” (t001619)

This shows **interactive negotiation of stance and activity structure**.

### d. Story‑telling and evaluation

The content of the excerpts is often **narrative or evaluative**:

- Recounting events (school race logistics, planning permission, holidays, birthday dinners, family disputes).
- Evaluating people and situations:
  - “some really arrogant guys but doing well for themselves” (t000989)
  - “some scruffians or whatever you wanna call them ruffians” (t001107)
  - “it just makes yourself it I dunno that's what makes her a target” (t000031)

The high‑loading items are embedded in **personal stories and opinionated talk**, not in task‑oriented or highly structured discourse.

### e. Spontaneous but sometimes serious topics

Even when the topic is serious (cancer experiences, Muslim attitudes to LGBT rights), the **delivery remains conversational and disfluent**:

- “we're gonna talk about body parts and stuff like that as well…” (t001643)
- “think about the vitriol that that's spur spewed on you by your own elected political leaders… are you okay with that?” (t001619)

So the dimension is not about “light vs serious” content; it’s about **how** the content is produced: spontaneously, with hesitations, colloquial forms, and interactive markers.

## 3. Who drives this pole?

- The **human group** has a much higher mean score than both GPT groups.
- All top‑scoring texts are **human**.
- The loading items (er, erm, wanna, cos, shit) are **typical of natural speech** and are generally under‑represented in LLM outputs, especially when models are prompted to be clear, fluent, and “well‑written”.

So this pole is **strongly human‑driven** and effectively captures **features that GPT does not reproduce at comparable density**, regardless of profiling.

---

## 4. Interpreting f2 (pos) as a discourse dimension

Bringing the evidence together:

- High loadings on **filled pauses** and **colloquial reductions**.
- Frequent **online planning**, **repairs**, and **hesitations**.
- **Informal, interpersonal narratives** and evaluations.
- **Discourse markers** managing turns and stance.
- Strongly associated with **human** rather than GPT turns.

This pole encodes a discourse style that is:

- **Spontaneous and unplanned** (speech produced in real time, with visible processing).
- **Informal and colloquial** (wanna, gonna, cos, shit).
- **Interpersonally engaged** (okay, somebody, narrative and evaluative content).
- **Authentically spoken** (dense “er/erm” and repairs).

## 5. Possible labels for f2 (positive pole)

Any label should foreground **spontaneous, informal, human conversational discourse** rather than contrasting it with the opposite pole. Suitable options:

1. **Spontaneous Informal Conversation**
   - Captures the unplanned, colloquial, interactional nature of the talk.

2. **Disfluent Colloquial Speech Production**
   - Emphasises the high density of hesitations and reduced forms as markers of real‑time production.

3. **Authentic Spoken Interaction Style**
   - Highlights that this is the human‑driven, naturally occurring conversational mode.

4. **Hesitant, Narrative Chat**
   - Brings in both the disfluency and the story‑telling, evaluative character of the excerpts.

Among these, for analytic clarity in LMDA terms, a concise primary label could be:

> **Spontaneous Informal Conversation**

with the understanding (justified above) that this encompasses **hesitant, disfluent, colloquial, human‑driven spoken interaction** as encoded by the high‑loading features and exemplified in the top‑scoring texts.
