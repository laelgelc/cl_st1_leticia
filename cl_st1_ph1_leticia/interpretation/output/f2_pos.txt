Interpretation of Factor 2 (positive pole)

1. **Which group drives this pole?**

- Mean scores:
  - **Profiled GPT**: +0.09  
  - Unprofiled GPT: +0.07  
  - Humans: −0.15  

The positive pole is clearly **LLM‑driven**, and especially by **profiled GPT** outputs. Human turns tend to sit on the opposite side of this factor.

So we are looking at a discourse style that the profiled GPT model over‑produces relative to humans.

2. **Key lexical loadings**

Positive loadings:

- **odd** (1.21)
- **chat** (.63)
- **bloke** (.44)
- **(sense** (.34) – presumably “sense” in collocations like “odd sense”, “sense that”, etc.

These are few but quite semantically coherent:

- **odd**: evaluative, marking something as unusual, inappropriate, or off.
- **chat / chatting**: informal talk, often reported or framed (“we just started chatting”, “that chat”).
- **bloke**: strongly colloquial, British, gendered reference to a man, often in morally or socially evaluative contexts.
- **sense** (by inference): often used to articulate a subjective stance (“it felt odd”, “I had a sense that…”).

3. **Patterns in the high‑scoring excerpts**

All 10 examples are **profiled GPT** texts. Across them, several consistent discourse features emerge:

a) **Narrated interpersonal episodes with a focal “odd” moment**

Each excerpt centres on a small story where something about another person’s behaviour is **socially or morally off**:

- t001777: receptionist ignoring the speaker; “nothing odd had happened”; “Asian woman… chatting away with this bloke”.
- t002508: administrator resigns at the last minute; “it was really odd, I went into the kitchen… and we just started chatting”.
- t001739: boyfriend’s story “didn’t add up”; “the odd casual message”; phone “wiped clean”.
- t003265: “this bloke” selling tickets; “sounded a bit odd”.
- t003343: man in court “immediately smiled and looked straight at me”; “felt a bit odd”.
- t003303: woman “stayed with that bloke who was horrible to women”; “this odd hope that [drinking] would make me feel better”.
- t002455: middle‑aged partner messaging a young lawyer on LinkedIn; “chatting her up”.
- t002559: “this bloke on the internet” trapping paedophiles.
- t001883: stranger acting like an old friend from just an email; “really odd”.
- t002955: conference networking; “this bloke who did that FCE talk”; “chatting to someone”; “felt a bit strange”.

So the positive pole is not just “informal chat” in general; it is **narrative recounting of socially awkward, morally questionable, or boundary‑crossing interactions**.

b) **Colloquial, British, relational lexis**

- **bloke** is frequent and central: “this bloke came up selling tickets”, “this middle‑aged bloke”, “this bloke on the internet”, “the same bloke who did that FCE talk”.
- **chat / chatting** is used to frame interaction:
  - “carried on chatting away with this bloke”
  - “we just started chatting”
  - “what happened with that chat”
  - “he’s chatting her up”
  - “I also ended up chatting to someone”
- The style is **spoken, informal, British**, with narrative sequencing (“then”, “and then eventually”, “in the end”, “within like two weeks”).

c) **Stance and evaluation of others’ behaviour**

The narrator repeatedly **evaluates** people and situations:

- “unprofessional”, “horrible to women”, “I just don’t buy [his story]”, “I really didn’t like it at all”.
- “it sounded a bit odd”, “felt a bit odd”, “it all felt a bit strange”.
- Suspicion and moral judgement: paedophiles, inappropriate LinkedIn messages, manipulative partners, neglectful staff.

The word **odd** is the clearest lexical marker of this stance: it flags **social norm violations or unease**.

d) **Focus on interpersonal boundaries and social norms**

Many episodes hinge on **breaches or tests of boundaries**:

- Professional vs personal (LinkedIn flirtation; administrator’s late resignation; conference networking).
- Age/power/gender dynamics (middle‑aged partner vs young lawyer; “bloke… horrible to women”; paedophile‑hunting).
- Stranger vs acquaintance (courtroom man staring; woman acting like an old friend from an email; ticket seller in Leicester Square).

The narrator positions themselves as **noticing and assessing** these boundary issues.

4. Integrating loadings and examples

- The **loadings** highlight a small but tight cluster: *odd* + *chat* + *bloke* (+ *sense*).  
- The **examples** show these items embedded in:
  - first‑person narratives,
  - about **other people’s behaviour**,
  - with **explicit stance** (unease, suspicion, moral evaluation),
  - in **informal British conversational style**.

This suggests that the factor is not simply “informal British talk” (that would likely include many more colloquialisms), but more specifically:

> A discourse mode of **evaluative, anecdotal talk about socially questionable or awkward interactions with (usually male) others**, framed as “odd” and recounted through “chats”.

5. Possible labels for the positive pole

Here are several candidate labels, each capturing a slightly different emphasis but grounded in the same pattern:

1. **“Evaluative Anecdotes about Socially ‘Odd’ Encounters”**  
   - Captures the narrative form (anecdotes) and the central evaluative term *odd*.

2. **“Informal Moral Commentary on Other People’s Behaviour”**  
   - Emphasises the stance: narrators judging others’ conduct in everyday situations.

3. **“Colloquial Narratives of Awkward or Inappropriate Social Interaction”**  
   - Highlights the interpersonal awkwardness and boundary issues.

4. **“British Conversational Stance: ‘Odd’ Blokes and Problematic Chats”**  
   - Makes explicit the British colloquial flavour (*bloke*, *chat*) and the evaluative stance (*odd*).

Any of these would be defensible. If a single label is needed, a concise synthesis that reflects both the lexical loadings and the examples is:

> **Colloquial Evaluative Narratives of ‘Odd’ Social Encounters**

This label:

- foregrounds **colloquial** (chat, bloke),
- foregrounds **evaluative** stance (odd, sense),
- foregrounds **narrative recounting** (the episodic stories),
- and foregrounds the **social‑interactional focus** (encounters, chats, blokes).

6. Role of source type

- The positive pole is **driven by profiled GPT** outputs, with unprofiled GPT somewhat similar and humans on the opposite side.
- This suggests that, when given socio‑demographic profiles, GPT tends to **over‑produce a stylised British conversational persona** that:
  - uses *bloke* and *chat*,
  - foregrounds “odd” social situations,
  - and narrates them with explicit stance.

So, as a discourse dimension, f2_pos can be interpreted as:

> A GPT‑amplified, British‑style, colloquial mode of telling short, evaluative stories about “odd” or questionable social interactions, especially involving “blokes” and “chats”.
