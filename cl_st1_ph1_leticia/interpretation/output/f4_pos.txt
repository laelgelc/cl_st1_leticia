Interpretation of Factor 4 (positive pole)

1. Which sources drive this pole?

- Mean scores:
  - profiled_gpt: 0.83  
  - unprofiled_gpt: 0.80  
  - human: 0.33  

All three groups are on the positive side, but both GPT conditions are substantially higher than humans, with profiled_gpt slightly leading. So this pole is strongly LLM‑driven, especially by profiled GPT outputs, but it is not an artefact of GPT alone: humans also pattern positively, just less extremely.

2. Lexical loadings: what kind of discourse is this?

High positive loadings:

- Strongest:
  - push (.53)
  - (least (.47))
  - bother (.45)
  - feel (.39)
  - stick (.37)
- Also:
  - clear (.35)
  - carry (.34)
  - (more (.34))
  - (way (.34))
  - expect (.32)
  - make (.32)
  - (everyone (.31))
  - head (.30)

Several things stand out:

- Mental / affective stance:
  - feel, head, bother  
  These index internal states, evaluation, and cognitive processing (“in my head”, “I feel”, “doesn’t bother me”).

- Managing responsibility and effort:
  - push, carry, expect, make, bother  
  These are about exerting effort, placing demands, or refusing to expend effort (“push them”, “carry them”, “expect her to manage”, “wouldn’t bother with maths”).

- Stability / persistence:
  - stick  
  Often used in “stick to” rules, systems, or patterns.

- Clarity and structure:
  - clear, way, more, everyone  
  “Make it clear”, “clear boundaries”, “the way things have turned out”, “everyone” as a generalized participant.

Overall, the loadings point to a discourse where speakers:

- Explicitly articulate how they feel and think.
- Reflect on who should do what (responsibility, expectations).
- Emphasise clarity, rules, and structure.
- Talk about being stuck or needing to “stick” to a system or pattern.

3. What do the high‑scoring excerpts actually do?

Across the 10 examples, the same pattern recurs. I’ll summarise the discourse functions rather than go excerpt by excerpt.

a) Metacognitive and affective self‑explanation

Many texts are extended reflections on internal states, often contrasting:

- what they “feel” vs. what they “think” or “expect”
- past vs. present feelings
- what “should” be the case vs. what actually is

Examples:

- “it wasn’t because everything was suddenly sorted in my head… it was the guilt I’d been carrying eased off… the improvement was more about reduced guilt than a strong, clear desire to come back” (t002096)
- “I feel like I’ve got no right to kick off… that’s why this is doing my head in… I’m stuck between not wanting to be a cow and actually wanting a bit more from him” (t003368)
- “when something doesn’t make sense or won’t stick in my head, I feel different from the confident person I once was” (t002874)

These are not just emotional outbursts; they are analytical, unpacking feelings, causes, and contradictions.

b) Negotiating responsibility, expectations, and agency

Several excerpts explicitly discuss who should carry responsibility, how much effort is reasonable, and what others can fairly be expected to do:

- Teaching / classroom management:
  - “I made it clear I was only interested in how they behaved in my lessons… I’d push them: ‘Right then, if you know the difference, you don’t play up for me, agreed?’” (t001979)
  - “I just don’t connect with the way I’m expected to teach… I end up feeling a bit lost, as if everything depends on me improvising and carrying them. I don’t think it should be like that; the responsibility should sit more with the learners.” (t002681)
  - “she was so vulnerable, and it felt completely inappropriate to expect her to manage on her own like that… as long as you gave her clear boundaries and routines she responded really well, she knew what was expected” (t001779)

- Relationships:
  - “I feel like I’ve got no right to kick off… I’m stuck between not wanting to be a cow and actually wanting a bit more from him” (t003368)
  - “there doesn’t have to be a villain here… the only way either of us gets any peace is by letting go of the guilt and that way of thinking” (t002099)
  - “I honestly thought if we just sat down and talked, we could clear the air and then carry on as friends… All of that has always made me feel like he thought I was a bit over the top… So hearing him suddenly say he loved me… really threw me.” (t002930)

Responsibility is constantly being allocated, resisted, or renegotiated: who should behave, who should manage, who should feel guilty, who should carry whom.

c) Emphasis on clarity, structure, and “making things clear”

Several texts explicitly foreground making things clear, setting boundaries, or having a system:

- “I always divide [the A4 sheet] into three sections… so she’s got a clear record… The idea is that the sheet makes sense to them, not that it’s technically perfect.” (t002025)
- “There aren’t those really engaging… materials that guide the students through and make the learning objectives clear and motivating.” (t002681)
- “I made it clear I was only interested in how they behaved in my lessons.” (t001979)
- “as long as you gave her clear boundaries and routines she responded really well, she knew what was expected” (t001779)
- “I honestly thought if we just sat down and talked, we could clear the air and then carry on as friends” (t002930)

So clarity is not just a lexical item; it is a discourse goal: explaining, structuring, and making expectations explicit.

d) Being “stuck”, “carrying” things, and cognitive load

The verbs stick, carry, head, bother, push often appear in metaphorical or psychological senses:

- “the only real ‘system’ I stick to is my A4 sheet” (t002025)
- “this is doing my head in a bit” (t003368)
- “a lot of the guilt I’d been carrying eased off” (t002096)
- “the whole guilt thing… keeps us stuck” (t002099)
- “when something doesn’t make sense or won’t stick in my head” (t002874)
- “I end up feeling a bit lost, as if everything depends on me improvising and carrying them” (t002681)

These suggest a discourse of cognitive and emotional load: burdens carried, things that won’t “stick” mentally, situations that “do your head in”, and the need to push or not bother.

4. How do loadings and examples converge?

If we align the lexical loadings with the discourse functions in the examples, we get a coherent picture:

- feel, head, bother → explicit talk about internal states, confusion, distress, reluctance to expend effort.
- push, carry, expect, make, everyone → negotiation of effort, obligation, and distributed responsibility across participants.
- stick, clear, way, more, least → orientation to stable systems, clear rules, and “the way” things are or should be.

Crucially, this is not just “emotional talk”; it is reflective, often quasi‑explanatory discourse about:

- how one feels,
- why one feels that way,
- what should be expected of self and others,
- and how to structure situations to make them manageable or fair.

5. Role of human vs. LLM groups

- All high‑scoring examples provided are from profiled_gpt.
- Mean scores show:
  - profiled_gpt ≈ unprofiled_gpt >> human.

So the LLM, especially under the profiled prompt, amplifies this style: long, introspective, carefully structured explanations of feelings, expectations, and responsibilities. Humans also use this discourse, but less intensely and less consistently.

This suggests that the factor is not purely an artefact of synthetic text, but GPT outputs—especially when given socio‑demographic profiles—tend to produce highly elaborated versions of this reflective, responsibility‑oriented discourse.

6. Possible labels for f4 (positive pole)

Any label should capture:

- explicit expression and analysis of feelings and thoughts;
- negotiation of responsibility, expectations, and effort;
- emphasis on clarity, structure, and “making things clear”;
- a sense of cognitive/emotional load (guilt, being stuck, carrying burdens).

Plausible labels:

1. **Reflective Responsibility and Emotional Load Discourse**

   - “Reflective” for the metacognitive, explanatory treatment of feelings and situations.
   - “Responsibility” for the recurrent negotiation of who should do what, who should carry whom, and what is expected.
   - “Emotional Load” for guilt, worry, feeling stuck, and burdens being carried or released.

2. **Metacognitive Stance and Expectation‑Management Discourse**

   - “Metacognitive stance” for the explicit talk about what’s “in my head”, how things “feel”, and how they “stick”.
   - “Expectation‑management” for the strong focus on what is or isn’t appropriate to expect of students, partners, vulnerable people, etc.

3. **Clarity‑Seeking, Guilt‑ and Burden‑Negotiating Discourse**

   - “Clarity‑seeking” for the repeated drive to make things clear, set boundaries, clear the air.
   - “Guilt‑ and burden‑negotiating” for the way guilt, responsibility, and emotional burdens are allocated, resisted, or let go.

Any of these captures the same underlying dimension. If I had to choose a single compact label for this pole:

> **Reflective Responsibility and Emotional Load**

This foregrounds both the introspective, explanatory stance and the central themes of responsibility, expectations, and carried/relieved burdens that are consistently realised in the loadings and the high‑scoring texts, and it aligns with the LLM‑driven tendency to produce extended, carefully structured self‑explanations of how things feel and who should be responsible.
