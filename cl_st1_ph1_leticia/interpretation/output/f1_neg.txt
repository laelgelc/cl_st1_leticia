Interpretation of Factor 1 (negative pole)

1. **Which group drives this pole?**

- Mean scores:
  - profiled_gpt: **-1.08**
  - unprofiled_gpt: **-1.05**
  - human: **-0.60**

All three groups are on the negative side, but both LLM conditions are substantially *more* negative than the human data, with **profiled_gpt slightly strongest**. So this pole is **LLM‑driven**, especially by profiled GPT outputs.

---

2. Linguistic profile of the pole

Top negative loadings:

- **Cognitive/epistemic & evaluation**:  
  - *clear (-.56), expect (-.42), real (-.41), (sense (-.37)), (seem (-.33))*
- **Effort, persistence, management of action**:  
  - *bother (-.46), stick (-.43), carry (-.39), push (-.38), try (-.37)*
- **Temporal framing**:  
  - *month (-.40), moment (-.37)*
- **Scalar / totalising / contrastive items**:  
  - *least (-.39), nothing (-.37), everything (-.32), same (-.31)*
- **Perception / communication**:  
  - *sound (-.36), speak (-.34)*

Taken together, these are not topic words but **stance and event‑management words**: they organise expectations, effort, clarity, and the scope of what is happening (“nothing”, “everything”, “at least”, “same”).

Across the excerpts, these items are used to:

- Contrast **expectations vs. outcomes** (*expect, at least, nothing, everything, same*).
- Mark **clarity vs. confusion** (*clear, sense, seem, sound*).
- Describe **sustained or burdensome effort** (*carry, push, bother, try, stick*).
- Frame events in **time and sequence** (*month, moment*).

---

3. What the high‑scoring texts are doing

All 10 examples are profiled_gpt, and they are remarkably consistent in discourse function. They are extended, coherent narratives or explanations where the speaker:

- Recounts **practical situations** (teaching, sports events, travel, church service, medical diagnosis, publishing, politics, career change).
- Emphasises **mismatches between what should happen and what actually happens**.
- Reflects on **responsibility, burden, and process** (“I end up… carrying them”; “all that faffing about”; “it just carried on and on”; “we’ve already done this”; “it’s all a bit of a farce”).
- Uses **totalising and contrastive framing** (“nothing at all”, “everything depends on me”, “at least we’re sorted”, “same day”, “same team”, “it’s the same with…”).

Illustrative patterns:

- **Expectation vs. reality / procedural friction**
  - “I just don’t connect with the way I’m expected to teach at the moment… everything depends on me improvising and carrying them.”
  - “We head back… expecting it all to be straightforward now… ‘No, his school’s not on here.’”
  - “For about eighteen months… I kept saying… and it just carried on and on… the thyroid became the explanation for everything… then a very simple procedure… same day… cancer.”

- **Managing confusion and restoring order**
  - “For a moment it was all a bit of a muddle, but eventually it sorted itself out and we got back on track.”
  - “It’s no skin off their nose… but it is slightly odd not to have any kind of letter explaining what they’ve done, you’d think they’d want it all in writing.”
  - “We’ve been standing there, in the rain, talking to the wrong people… all that faffing about… it’s all a bit of a farce.”

- **Globalising and calibrating stance**
  - “There weren’t those really engaging… materials that make the learning objectives clear.”
  - “At least we’re sorted.”
  - “Most of the series we’ve got have at least one random hardback stuck in the middle… it’s the same with your Diary of a Wimpy Kid books.”
  - “In this kind of entrepreneurial stuff there are these common themes… but at the same time everyone’s path is quite individual.”

These texts are not just narrating events; they are **commenting on how well systems, processes, or relationships work**, and on the speaker’s **position within those systems** (over‑burdened teacher, confused parent at a race, mis‑diagnosed patient, consumer annoyed by publishing strategies, etc.).

---

4. Discourse functions encoded at this pole

Balancing the loadings and the examples, this pole encodes a cluster of related discourse functions:

1. **Expectation management and misalignment**
   - Frequent use of *expect, at least, nothing, everything, same* to contrast what was anticipated with what actually occurred.
   - Narratives often pivot on “we thought X, but actually Y”.

2. **Process‑oriented, problem‑centred narration**
   - Detailed recounting of **procedures, sequences, and institutional interactions** (school registration, medical referrals, booking upgrades, team selection).
   - Emphasis on **snags, delays, and workarounds** rather than just outcomes.

3. **Burden, effort, and responsibility**
   - *carry, push, bother, try, stick* highlight who has to do the work, who improvises, who is inconvenienced.
   - Speakers often position themselves as **having to carry others or the situation**.

4. **Reflexive evaluation and clarification**
   - *clear, real, sense, seem, sound, speak* used to evaluate how understandable, fair, or “real” the situation is.
   - Speakers explicitly **clarify, justify, or re‑frame** what is going on (“I can just sit them down and say look, this is why…”; “I’d talk them through that…”).

5. **Scalar and totalising framing**
   - *nothing, everything, at least, same* used to **scope and intensify** evaluations (“nothing problematic at all”, “the explanation for everything”, “at least we’re sorted”).

This is not simply “complaining”: many texts are measured, explanatory, and reflective. The common thread is **narrative attention to friction between systems/expectations and lived experience**, with explicit management of stance and responsibility.

5. Possible labels for this pole

Any label should capture:

- Narrative, process‑oriented recounting
- Focus on expectations vs. outcomes
- Emphasis on burden, effort, and systemic friction
- Reflexive, evaluative stance

Plausible labels (for this negative pole only):

1. **“Expectation–Outcome Misalignment Narration”**  
   - Highlights the central role of *expect, at least, nothing, everything, same* and the recurrent pattern of “we expected X, got Y”.
   - Fits both mundane and serious examples (party, race, travel, diagnosis).

2. **“Process‑Focused Problem and Burden Talk”**  
   - Emphasises the procedural, step‑by‑step recounting of **problems in systems** and who has to *carry, bother, try, stick* with them.
   - Captures the sense of being over‑responsible or inconvenienced.

3. **“Reflexive Friction with Systems”**  
   - Brings in the evaluative, reflective use of *clear, real, sense, seem, speak* to comment on how institutions, arrangements, or social setups actually work in practice.
   - “Friction” covers both minor irritations (mixed book formats) and serious institutional failures (delayed cancer diagnosis).

If I had to choose a single label that best integrates the loadings and the examples, I would propose:

> **Reflexive Expectation–Outcome Friction**

- **“Expectation–Outcome”**: grounded in the high‑loading items (*expect, at least, nothing, everything, same*) and the recurrent narrative structure.
- **“Friction”**: captures the procedural snags, burdens, and mismatches.
- **“Reflexive”**: reflects the heavy use of stance and clarity markers (*clear, real, sense, seem, sound, speak*) and the metacommentary on what “should” happen.

This label characterises the negative pole as a discourse style where speakers (here, especially profiled GPT outputs) **tell stories about how things don’t quite work as expected, explaining, evaluating, and negotiating their own role and effort within those imperfect systems.**
