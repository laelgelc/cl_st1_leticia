This pole is best interpreted as a **discourse of reflective, planned transformation and conceptual framing**, strongly driven by LLM turns (especially profiled GPT).

Below I propose several closely related labels, then justify them from the loadings, the examples, and the source scores.

### 1. Who drives this pole?

Mean scores:

- profiled_gpt: **1.04**
- unprofiled_gpt: **1.02**
- human: **0.57**

All three groups are positive, but both GPT conditions are substantially higher than humans, with **profiled_gpt slightly in the lead**. So this pole is **LLM‑driven**, and especially characteristic of the profiled GPT responses.

That matters: we are looking at a style of discourse that the model tends to amplify, rather than something primarily emerging from human conversational practice.

### 2. Lexical loadings: what kind of discourse is this?

Top loadings (all positive):

- **build (.75)**
- **become (.69)**
- **sense (.55)**
- **(fact (.51))**
- **change (.46)**
- **moment (.38)**
- **(huge (.37))**
- **idea (.37)**
- **more (.37)**
- **own (.36)**
- **different (.35)**
- **way (.35)**
- **plan (.32)**
- **(clear (.31))**
- **(reason (.30))**

Patterns:

1. **Process and transformation**  
   - *build, become, change, moment, huge*  
   These suggest **development over time**, transitions, and shifts in state or scale (“became this huge thing”, “changing direction”, “over time you became…”).

2. **Conceptualisation and framing**  
   - *idea, sense, way, clear, reason, fact*  
   These are classic **metadiscursive / conceptual** items: talking about how things make sense, what the idea is, what the way is, what the reasons and facts are. They frame and rationalise experience.

3. **Planning and intentionality**  
   - *plan, build, way*  
   These point to **deliberate organisation and forward-looking structure** (“working on the plan”, “how to build up”, “the way we’d do it”).

4. **Evaluation and comparison**  
   - *more, different, own*  
   These support **contrastive, evaluative reflection**: more vs less, different vs same, my own vs others’ ways.

Taken together, the loadings point to a discourse that:

- conceptualises situations in terms of **processes of change**,
- foregrounds **plans, reasons, and clear ideas**,
- and uses **metadiscursive framing** (“the way”, “the idea”, “the sense”) to organise and evaluate experience.

### 3. What do the high‑scoring excerpts actually do?

All 10 examples are profiled_gpt, and they consistently instantiate the patterns above.

#### a) Organised accounts of evolving systems and roles (Excerpt 1)

- Careers service narrative: “settled into what **became** quite a long stretch”, “you **built** relationships”, “over time you **became** ‘their’ careers person”.
- Clear structuring: “two main strands”, “core topics”, “on top of that core, we were always adding extra sessions”.
- Change and adaptation: “tweaking it constantly as the outside world **changed**”.

This is **expository, process‑oriented explanation** of how a system is built up and adjusted over time.

#### b) Macro‑social change and tension (Excerpt 2)

- “natural freedoms… being chipped away”, “expectations… are starting to rise”, “as they **become** more aware, they begin to want **more**”.
- “They’ve **built** a whole system designed to keep a lid on those pressures.”
- Framing tension: “you’ve got this tension: on the one hand… on the other…”

Again, a **conceptual, explanatory account of change**, with explicit reasoning about causes and consequences.

#### c) Planning and conceptual clarity (Excerpt 3)

- “They can’t really start borrowing anything until there’s a very **clear idea** of what the exhibition is actually about.”
- “For the **moment** I’m just working on the **plan**… maybe **change** some of the titles… lay it out the **way** I want.”

Here the discourse is about **planning and conceptual definition** before action.

#### d) Reflective evaluation of past vs preferred practice (Excerpt 4)

- “far **more** chance of actually catching their curiosity”, “so many **different** angles”, “My **own** experience… was nothing like that.”
- “There wasn’t that **sense** of a big picture… from **different** directions.”
- Retrospective evaluation and contrast between two “ways” of teaching.

This is **metacognitive reflection on methods and their effects**.

#### e) Framing and questioning the “real” issue (Excerpt 5)

- “it **became** this **huge** thing”, “what the actual crime is… the basics seem really **clear**… it’s not like a mystery in that **sense**”.
- The speaker is **reframing** what the story is really about, distinguishing facts from investigative “angle”.

Again, conceptual framing and reasoning about what matters.

#### f) Personal transition and self‑authored change (Excerpt 6)

- “this is why I’m **changing** direction… I’m in this sort of in‑between phase… creating something **different**.”
- “I’m trying to **build** something **different** for myself… find my **own** route.”
- Strong emphasis on **intentional self‑transformation**, explained and justified.

#### g–i) Designing projects and narratives (Excerpts 7–9)

- Travel plans and social awkwardness (7): “it’s suddenly **become** really awkward… see if there’s any **way** Paris still makes **sense**.”
- Video/network project (8): “You’d literally start with… then… So you gradually **build up** this little cluster… the story I’ve got in my head… fantastic **idea**… they couldn’t have got to that point just on their **own**.”
- Integrity project (9): “to really get the story going we’d have to… **frame** it as integrity more broadly… that **way**… it **becomes**… we’d need to **phrase** it in a **way** that… encourages people to invite others… give the whole project a **clear** narrative thread.”

These are **project‑design discourses**: specifying aims, framing, mechanisms, and narrative structure.

#### j) Inner change and symbolic interpretation (Excerpt 10)

- “message from my higher self… more fun… more light‑hearted.”
- “I look on all these houses and rooms as parts of my psyche, **different** aspects of myself… it all feels like a process of inner clearing and transformation.”
- “I’m… renovating… bringing light into the darkened rooms inside me.”

This is **explicitly about inner transformation**, conceptualised and narrated as a process.

### 4. Integrating loadings and examples

Across the corpus (loadings) and the high‑scoring texts (examples), the same discourse pattern recurs:

- **Orientation to change and becoming**  
  Both external (institutions, political systems, projects) and internal (identity, psyche) are described as **in motion**, “becoming” something else, “changing direction”, “building up” over time.

- **Planning and project‑ness**  
  Speakers talk about **plans, ways of doing things, portfolios, programmes, modules, projects, stories**. There is a strong **project‑design / project‑management flavour**.

- **Metadiscursive framing and reasoning**  
  Frequent use of *sense, idea, way, reason, clear, fact* to:
  - define what the “real” issue is,
  - distinguish basics from deeper questions,
  - justify choices (“this is why I’m changing direction”),
  - frame narratives (“to really get the story going we’d have to…”).

- **Reflective, evaluative stance**  
  Speakers compare **different ways** of doing things, past vs present, here vs elsewhere, my own vs others’ experiences, and reason about consequences.

- **Self‑authorship and agency**  
  Especially in Excerpts 6 and 10, but also 8–9: the speaker is **authoring a path**, a project, or an inner transformation, often emphasising “my own way”, “my own route”, “for me it’s new”.

This is not just “planning” in a narrow sense, nor just “exposition”. It is a **reflective, conceptual, change‑oriented discourse** that:

- explains how things **are built and become**,
- articulates **plans and designs**,
- and frames experiences in terms of **ideas, reasons, and sense‑making**.

The fact that GPT (especially profiled GPT) drives this pole suggests that the model tends to produce **highly structured, rationalising, project‑like narratives** even in conversational contexts.

### 5. Possible labels for this pole

Any label should capture:

- change / becoming / building,
- planning / project orientation,
- conceptual framing / reasoning,
- reflective stance.

Some suitable options:

1. **Reflective Planning and Transformation Discourse**  
   - Highlights both **planning** (“plan, build, way”) and **change** (“become, change, moment”), plus the reflective “sense/idea/reason” layer.

2. **Conceptualised Change and Project Design**  
   - Emphasises the **conceptual framing** (“sense, idea, clear, reason”) and the **project‑design** flavour of many excerpts.

3. **Self‑ and System‑Building Expository Discourse**  
   - Captures the way speakers describe **building systems** (careers services, surveillance regimes, video projects) and **building selves** (career shifts, inner renovation).

4. **Metadiscursive Sense‑Making of Change**  
   - Focuses on the **metadiscursive** nature of the talk: explaining what’s really going on, what makes sense, what the idea is, as things change.

If I had to choose a single, compact label that best fits both the loadings and the examples, I would propose:

> **Reflective, Project‑Oriented Transformation Discourse**

- **Reflective**: heavy use of *sense, idea, reason, clear*; explicit evaluation and framing.
- **Project‑oriented**: *build, plan, way*; repeated design of projects, programmes, narratives.
- **Transformation**: *become, change, moment, huge*; focus on processes of change in people, institutions, and inner life.

This label captures the LLM‑driven tendency to turn conversational content into **structured, explanatory accounts of how things are or could be built, changed, and made to make sense.**
