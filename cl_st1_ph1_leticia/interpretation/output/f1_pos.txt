This factor is extremely narrow: it is driven almost entirely by the discourse marker / hesitation filler **“um”**, and the high-scoring texts are all **human** turns. Both GPT conditions have substantially *lower* mean scores than humans, so humans are clearly driving this pole.

### 1. Source scores: who drives the pole?

Mean f1 scores:

- Human: **-0.60**
- Unprofiled GPT: **-1.05**
- Profiled GPT: **-1.08**

Higher scores = more of the feature. All three groups are on the negative side overall, but humans are **closest to zero**, i.e. they use **more “um”** than either GPT condition. The LLM outputs are more “disfluent-free” and thus sit further from the positive pole.

So this pole is essentially a **human-spoken, disfluent** end of the dimension, with GPT turns clustering away from it.

### 2. Loadings: what linguistic feature defines the pole?

Only one variable loads on f1_pos:

- **um (1.71)**

No other lexical items co-load, so this factor is not a broad semantic or stylistic cluster; it is a **pure hesitation-marker factor**.

### 3. What do the high-scoring excerpts show?

All 10 high-scoring texts are:

- Human-authored
- Spontaneous, informal conversational narratives or explanations
- Rich in **“um”** as a hesitation / planning device

In the excerpts, “um” appears:

- Turn-initial:  
  - “**um** but you know that was fairly standard Magnum fair…” (t001705)  
- Clause-medial, marking planning or repair:  
  - “we just **um** had a bite quick bite to eat” (t000094)  
  - “the fruit trees that grow along the wall **um** they're a very good idea…” (t000350)  
- As a soft boundary between discourse units or topic shifts:  
  - “you get good ideas going round **um** garden centres round the **um** the kitchen gardens…” (t000348)  
- As a stance/engagement softener:  
  - “I know well they had their they had like a **um** a chip like a bike their bike lock…” (t000695)

Across the examples, “um” functions to:

- Buy **planning time** in complex narratives (insurance procedures, TV plot, job loss, civil rights history).
- Signal **on-line formulation** and self-repair (“er… um…”, “I was like… um…”).
- Maintain **floor-holding** while the speaker searches for words or restructures the clause.
- Soften or hedge upcoming content in personal stories and evaluations.

Crucially, the surrounding discourse is not systematically of one topic or register (we have gardening, TV, work, music, history, Old English). The commonality is **spontaneous, cognitively demanding talk** with visible on-line processing.

### 4. Interpreting the discourse dimension

Because:

- The factor is defined solely by **“um”**,
- The high-scoring texts are **human, spontaneous, narrative/explanatory** turns,
- GPT outputs are systematically **lower** on this factor (they avoid “um”),

this pole is best interpreted as a **discourse-pragmatic** dimension of **on-line, disfluent, planning-in-progress talk**.

It is not about topic, formality, or stance per se; it is about **production mode** and **cognitive processing visibility**:

- **High on this pole**:  
  - Human-like, **unrehearsed** speech  
  - Frequent **filled pauses** marking hesitation, planning, and repair  
  - Strong sense of “thinking aloud” and **incremental formulation**

- **Low on this pole** (where GPT sits):  
  - More **edited / fluent** production  
  - Absence of filled pauses; planning is not lexically marked  
  - Less overt evidence of on-line processing

### 5. Possible labels for this positive pole

Focusing on this pole only, and capturing both the linguistic feature and its discourse function, suitable labels would be:

1. **On-line Planning and Hesitation Discourse**
   - Highlights that “um” indexes real-time planning and hesitation in talk.

2. **Spontaneous Disfluency / Filled-Pause Discourse**
   - Emphasises the disfluent, filled-pause character of the speech.

3. **Human-Like Hesitation and Floor-Holding**
   - Reflects that this is where human speakers mark hesitation and keep the floor.

Any of these captures the core: this pole encodes a **human-driven discourse style where speakers overtly manage planning, hesitation, and floor-holding through “um”**.
